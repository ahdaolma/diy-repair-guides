## Diagnose the Crack Type First

![Types of drywall cracks](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.zMUrty7p0ppOygwuFLAUmwHaF3%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=20e7e1aef4090760ff28f963340cffa11e0ec2289ca5761804904e73f437fc72&ipo=images)

Not all cracks are the same. Patching a structural crack with joint compound is like putting a bandage on a broken bone—it will reopen in weeks.

**Hairline crack along a taped seam (straight line, <1/16-inch wide):** The original drywall tape has failed. This is a cosmetic crack, not structural. The fix: cut out the old tape, retape, and mud. Permanent if done correctly.

**Corner crack (where wall meets wall or wall meets ceiling):** Usually caused by truss uplift in the attic—the roof framing lifts slightly with temperature changes, pulling the ceiling drywall away from the wall. These cracks are seasonal (wider in winter, narrower in summer). Fix with paper tape and a flexible setting-type compound (like Sheetrock 90). Do not use mesh tape in corners—it is too rigid and will crack again.

**Stair-step crack (diagonal, stepping along drywall edges):** This is a structural crack caused by foundation settlement or framing movement. Joint compound will not fix it. Before repairing the drywall, determine if the foundation movement is ongoing. If cracks are widening month over month, call a structural engineer. If the house is 20+ years old and the crack has been stable for years, you can repair it.

**Screw/nail pop crack (small circle or bump with crack radiating outward):** A drywall screw or nail has backed out. The fix: drive a new screw 2 inches above and below the pop, sink the popped screw deeper, and mud over both.

## Paper Tape vs Mesh Tape: The Real Difference

![Drywall tape application](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.9_zG5tpzqvAjmzI5cWAP9wHaEh%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=8a7577d77a6246cccf509c81325609c6b61846a6d6b4b4ca3b07d70925a40561&ipo=images)

This is the most important decision in crack repair, and 90% of DIYers get it wrong.

**Paper tape:** Requires bedding in a layer of all-purpose joint compound. The compound bonds the paper fibers to the drywall. Paper tape is stronger than mesh—it has a higher tensile strength and resists cracking better—but it is less forgiving. If you do not apply enough compound under the tape, it will bubble and fail. If you apply too much, you get a visible hump. Paper tape is the professional choice for seams and corner cracks.

**Mesh tape (FibaTape):** Self-adhesive—sticks directly to the drywall without a bedding coat. Easier to apply but structurally weaker. Mesh tape stretches slightly under stress, which means it allows the crack to telegraph through over time. Use mesh tape only for small patch repairs where the underlying structure is stable. Never use mesh tape in corners—the flex will cause the corner bead to crack.

**The third option most DIYers do not know about: Durabond.** For cracks that have been repaired before and failed, use Durabond 90 (a setting-type compound with high hardness and low shrinkage) for the first bedding coat, embed paper tape, then finish with lightweight compound. The Durabond base coat is harder than the surrounding drywall and will not crack. Available at any drywall supply store for about $15 per bag.

## The 3-Coat System for Permanent Crack Repair

![Drywall mud application](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.Ip7gl-cdBBE1Rw1zfTipQQAAAA%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=6177e2bea45a101d6bc4b1e772070c6b968f2c311be1d14007ba6ba4bf7c7fd7&ipo=images)

The #1 reason repaired cracks come back: one thick coat of mud instead of three thin ones. A single thick coat shrinks as it dries, pulling away from the tape and creating a new crack. Three thin coats, each allowed to dry fully, produce a repair stronger than the original drywall.

**Coat 1 (bedding coat):** Apply a thin, even layer of all-purpose joint compound (the green-lid stuff, not lightweight) with a 6-inch taping knife. Embed the paper tape into the wet compound. Press the tape flat with the knife, squeezing excess compound out from under it. The tape should be fully adhered with no bubbles. Let dry 24 hours.

**Coat 2 (fill coat):** Apply a wider coat (10-inch knife) of lightweight compound over the tape. Feather the edges—press harder on the outside edge of the knife so the compound tapers to nothing at the edge. This is the coat that hides the tape bump. Let dry 24 hours.

**Coat 3 (finish coat):** Apply a very thin skim coat with a 12-inch knife. This fills pinholes, knife marks, and minor imperfections. Sand lightly with 220-grit sandpaper between coats 2 and 3 for the smoothest result. Prime and paint after 48 hours total drying time.

## The Ceiling Crack Special Case

![Ceiling crack repair](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.Cdk2qAIP4Sd5fxB9AkilgwHaEl%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=058f6e2edad124566252e6d8650758cb74c94216b9c422116363e763afc4b802&ipo=images)

Ceiling cracks are harder to fix than wall cracks for two reasons: gravity pulls wet compound down, and ceiling cracks are often structural (truss uplift). Use these ceiling-specific tips:

1. Mix the compound slightly stiffer than normal—it should hold a peak, not slump.
2. Use setting-type compound (Sheetrock 90 or Durabond 90) for the bedding coat. It hardens by chemical reaction, not evaporation, so it does not shrink as much.
3. Screw the drywall to the joists on both sides of the crack before taping. Ceiling drywall often pulls away from the joists, and the crack is just the visible symptom. Drive 1-5/8-inch drywall screws every 8 inches along both sides of the crack into the joists.
4. After the repair is complete and painted, check it in the opposite season (summer repair → check in winter). If the crack returns, you have an active truss uplift problem and need an expansion joint, not another patch.

## The Moisture Test

![Drywall crack inspection](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.pyyUmwhTlC2EIBglOSj9wgHaFj%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=3fb359afc7fc6609fc8cee42f303430eb0e29afa17963fd7701664753048e137&ipo=images)

Before repairing any crack, hold a moisture meter against the drywall on both sides. If the reading is above 15%, you have an active leak—a roof leak for ceiling cracks, a plumbing leak for wall cracks. Repairing a crack without fixing the moisture source guarantees the repair will fail, and the mold growing inside the wall cavity will be your bigger problem. Fix the leak first, let the drywall dry completely (this can take 1-2 weeks with fans), then repair the crack.
