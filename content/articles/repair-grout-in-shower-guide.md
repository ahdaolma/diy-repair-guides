## When to Repair vs. Replace Shower Grout
![Removing old grout from shower tile with oscillating tool](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.odDPs3tL5V76hv4hw3MAPQHaHa%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=dfb8a9d91215746acff0831965b632f31845ad8352918ebd970a645670433489&ipo=images)

Grout fails in showers for three reasons: water penetration, movement, and improper installation. Cement-based grout is porous—water penetrates it, and behind the grout, the substrate (cement board, drywall, or mud bed) absorbs moisture. When the substrate swells, or when seasonal expansion and contraction cycles stress the grout, it cracks and crumbles. Once water gets behind the tile, the clock is ticking on substrate damage.

Repair grout when: cracks are hairline to ⅛ inch wide, crumbling is isolated to specific joints, and the tile itself is solidly attached (no movement when pressed). Replace the entire shower when: tiles are loose or hollow-sounding, the substrate is soft or water-damaged (push on the wall—if it gives, the substrate is gone), or mold has penetrated behind the tile and is growing through the grout.

## Step 1: Remove the Old Grout
![Applying new grout to shower tile](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.JhL1mqebj9VtLiGduhYP5gHaHa%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=db6def71785a8f7f1e08f81d0829afdf76173898897e3347bd532dd7a58a2ef9&ipo=images)

This is the most labor-intensive step. Grout must be removed to a depth of at least ⅔ the tile thickness—typically about ⅛ to ¼ inch—for new grout to have enough depth to bond. Skimming a thin layer of new grout over old grout will fail within weeks.

1. **Manual method (grout saw):** A grout saw is a handle with a carbide-grit blade. Scrape back and forth in the grout joint. This is slow and physically demanding but gives you full control and will not damage tile edges. Use for small areas (under 10 square feet of tile).
2. **Oscillating multi-tool with carbide grout blade:** This is the right tool for anything larger. A carbide or diamond grout removal blade ($10–15 for a multi-pack) fits a standard oscillating tool. The oscillating action removes grout quickly. Hold the tool at a consistent angle and move steadily along the joint. Wear safety glasses and a dust mask—this produces a blinding cloud of silica dust. Have a shop vacuum running with the nozzle held near the blade.
3. **Rotary tool (Dremel) with grout removal bit:** A Dremel with a carbide grout removal bit works for detail work around corners and fixtures where the oscillating tool cannot reach.

After removing the bulk of the grout, scrape the sides of the tiles with a utility knife to remove any remaining grout film. The edges must be clean for new grout to bond.

4. Vacuum all dust from the joints. Then wipe the joints with a damp sponge. The joints must be clean and damp (not wet) before grouting.

## Step 2: Choose the Right Grout

- **Unsanded grout:** For joints ⅛ inch or narrower. Used on wall tiles, polished stone, and any tile that scratches easily. Smoother texture, less shrinkage.
- **Sanded grout:** For joints ⅛ to ½ inch wide. The sand adds strength and prevents shrinkage cracking in wider joints. Used on floor tiles and most shower floors. Do not use on polished marble or glass tile—it will scratch the surface.
- **Epoxy grout:** Two-part epoxy that is waterproof, stainproof, and does not require sealing. Significantly harder to work with (sets in 30–45 minutes, cannot be cleaned with water after it begins to set). $30–50 per unit. Recommended for showers and wet areas if you are comfortable with the working time constraint.
- **Urethane grout:** Single-component, pre-mixed, waterproof, flexible. Easier to apply than epoxy. Fusion Pro and QuartzLock are common brands. $25–35 per gallon.

For most shower repairs, standard cement-based grout (sanded or unsanded as appropriate) with a high-quality penetrating sealer applied after curing provides good performance at the lowest cost.

## Step 3: Mix and Apply Grout
![Finished shower tile with new grout](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.WBI9tKJQgbW2VDA6sJmWoQHaHa%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=3895939636c4812ebf23be8efd11be56ddc506abe83e2c8148a2ec22cea0bd8a&ipo=images)

1. Mix grout powder with water per the package ratio. The consistency should be like creamy peanut butter—it should hold its shape on a trowel but spread easily. Mix only as much as you can apply in 30 minutes. Grout that begins to stiffen in the bucket cannot be revived by adding water.
2. Let the mixed grout slake (rest) for 5–10 minutes. This allows the dry polymers to fully hydrate. Then remix briefly.
3. Using a rubber grout float held at a 45-degree angle, press grout firmly into the joints. Work diagonally across the tiles—this prevents the float edge from dipping into the joints and pulling grout out.
4. Make multiple passes over each area, packing the joints completely full. There should be no voids, pinholes, or low spots. Hold the float at a steep angle (near 90 degrees) for the final pass to strike off excess grout from the tile faces.
5. Let the grout set for 15–30 minutes before the first sponge. Test by pressing the grout with a fingertip—it should be firm but not hard. If grout comes off on your finger, wait longer.

## Step 4: Sponge and Shape

1. Fill one bucket with clean water, another as the rinse bucket.
2. Wring the grout sponge until it is damp, not wet. A wet sponge introduces too much water into the fresh grout, weakening it and washing out the color.
3. Wipe across the tiles diagonally using light pressure in a circular motion. The goal is to smooth the grout joints and remove grout haze from the tile faces, not to dig grout out of the joints.
4. Rinse the sponge frequently—every 2–3 passes. Dirty sponges just move haze around.
5. After the first pass, let the grout set for another 15–30 minutes. A light haze will form on the tiles.
6. Second pass: wipe with a clean, damp sponge to remove the haze. At this point, the joints should be smooth and consistent, and the tile faces should be mostly clean.
7. After 2–3 hours, when a dry powder haze appears, buff the tiles with a dry microfiber cloth or cheesecloth. This removes the final haze without water.

## Step 5: Cure and Seal

Cement-based grout must cure for 48–72 hours before exposure to water. Do not use the shower during this period. After curing, apply a penetrating grout sealer:

1. Ensure the grout is completely dry (at least 72 hours after application).
2. Apply sealer with a small brush or applicator bottle directly to the grout joints. Do not coat the entire tile—sealer on impervious tile surfaces (ceramic, porcelain) does not penetrate and leaves a sticky residue.
3. Wipe any sealer that gets on the tile face off immediately with a dry cloth.
4. Apply a second coat after the first has absorbed (typically 30–60 minutes).
5. Allow 24 hours for the sealer to cure before water exposure.
6. Reapply sealer annually. Test if sealer is still effective by sprinkling a few drops of water on the grout—if they bead up, the seal is intact. If they soak in and darken the grout, reseal.

## Prevention

- Run the bathroom exhaust fan for 20–30 minutes after showering. Reduced humidity means slower grout deterioration and less mold.
- Wipe standing water off horizontal surfaces (shower curb, bench, floor perimeter) after each use.
- Inspect grout annually. Hairline cracks filled immediately with grout color-matched caulk ($8–10) prevent them from expanding and requiring full regrouting.
- Do not use acidic cleaners (vinegar, CLR, tile cleaners containing phosphoric or sulfamic acid) on cement grout. They etch and weaken the grout surface. Use pH-neutral stone and tile cleaner.
