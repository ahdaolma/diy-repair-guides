## Why Prep Work Determines the Result
![Painter cutting in along ceiling edge with brush](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.7bdbKWGnLk9f3rSh5a1rogHaEK%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=ce8da1bfb6a1d70dabe3f50e884c07b2a9ec1cafbf635ee527a4c17bd149d609&ipo=images)

Professional painters spend roughly 50–60% of their time on prep work and 40–50% on actual painting. DIYers typically reverse that ratio—and the results show it. Drips, roller marks, uneven coverage, paint on trim, and peeling within a year are all prep failures, not painting failures. According to a survey by Sherwin-Williams, inadequate surface preparation is cited as the cause in over 70% of paint failure complaints. The paint itself is almost never the problem.

This guide covers the full process: prep, cutting in, rolling, and cleanup. The materials for painting a 12×12 room cost approximately $75–$125. Professional painting of the same room costs $350–$600.

## Surface Preparation

![Wall being rolled with paint roller](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.explicit.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.rXIteOb6YEHLX9Ufun55DgHaHa%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=1ca7029c7a09706c91a75b8fe5cd6ef04e29160d5718d006f5597d42b3533231&ipo=images)

1. **Remove everything from the walls.** Outlet covers, switch plates, curtain rods, picture hooks, nails. Put the screws back into the outlet after removing the cover so you do not lose them. Cover outlets and switches with painter's tape to keep paint out.
2. **Fill holes and dings.** Spackle every nail hole, screw hole, and ding. A 12×12 room typically has 20–40 nail holes from previous picture hanging. Each takes 10 seconds to fill. Let dry, sand flush.
3. **Wash the walls.** Use a solution of warm water and a small amount of TSP (trisodium phosphate) or a TSP substitute. Walls accumulate a film of cooking grease, dust, skin oils, and previous cleaner residue. Paint does not adhere to grease. A sponge mop makes wall washing fast—no ladder needed for standard 8-foot ceilings. Rinse with clean water and let dry.
4. **Sand glossy surfaces.** If the existing paint is glossy or semi-gloss, sand lightly with 150-grit to create mechanical adhesion for the new paint. Wipe off sanding dust with a damp cloth.
5. **Prime where necessary.** Prime bare drywall patches, water stains (use a stain-blocking primer like Kilz or Zinsser BIN for these—standard primer will not block water stains, which will bleed through), and walls going from dark to light color. One coat of primer plus two coats of paint produces better coverage and durability than three coats of paint alone.
6. **Tape.** Apply painter's tape to baseboards, door and window trim, crown molding, and ceiling edges where you will cut in. Press the tape edge down firmly with a putty knife or your fingernail—the edge is where paint bleeds through. Remove tape while the paint is still slightly wet (within an hour of the final coat) for the cleanest line. If paint dries on the tape, it can peel the new paint off when removed.

## Cutting In: The Most Important Skill

![Finished interior wall with clean paint lines](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.PgEMw6sLa2mfthgH3-LlEQHaEL%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=05cacf000e27ae09693d056af461d68766f3c3a0084da4bc48153fcf075db50e&ipo=images)

Cutting in means painting a 2–3 inch wide band along all edges—where walls meet ceiling, trim, corners, and around outlets—using a brush, before filling the large areas with a roller. The roller cannot get within 2 inches of these edges, and the brush band provides a wet edge for the roller to blend into.

Technique for a straight cut line:

1. Load the brush: dip bristles about ½ to ⅔ of the way into the paint. Tap the brush against the side of the can—do not wipe it on the rim, which removes too much paint and pushes paint into the ferrule (the metal band), making cleanup harder.
2. Start about ½ inch away from the edge you are painting to. Paint a short stroke, then back-brush over it to spread the paint evenly.
3. Now approach the edge: with a steady hand, draw the brush along the edge in one smooth, continuous stroke. The bristles should fan out slightly against the surface, not bend sharply. Do not stop mid-stroke—stops create blobs. At the end of the stroke, lift the brush while still moving to feather the paint edge.
4. Paint 2–3 strokes parallel to the edge to widen the band to 2–3 inches.
5. Cut in an entire wall before rolling it. The cut-in band must still be wet when you roll—if it dries, you will get a visible texture difference called a "hat band" or picture-frame effect.

Cut in the ceiling edge first, then corners, then baseboards, then around trim. If cutting in feels unsteady, brace your painting hand with your other hand or rest your elbow on a stable surface.

## Rolling Technique

1. Fit the roller cover onto the frame, attach the extension pole. Adjust the pole so you can roll from floor to ceiling without bending or reaching overhead.
2. Fill the tray: pour paint into the deep end of the roller tray to a depth of about ½ inch.
3. Load the roller: roll it through the paint, then roll it back and forth on the textured ramp of the tray 4–5 times to distribute paint evenly through the nap. The roller should be saturated but not dripping. A properly loaded roller covers roughly one 3×3 foot area per dip.
4. Apply to the wall in a W or N pattern: starting about a foot from the edge, roll a large W or N shape about 3 feet wide. Then, without lifting the roller, fill in the gaps with parallel vertical strokes, overlapping each stroke by about 50%. The W pattern distributes paint evenly and prevents the roller from leaving a heavy deposit where it first touches the wall.
5. Finish each section with light, parallel strokes from ceiling to floor, all in the same direction. This "laying off" aligns the paint stipple in one direction for a uniform appearance.
6. Maintain a wet edge: each new roller load should overlap the previous section by 2–3 inches while the previous section is still wet. If you let an edge dry and then roll over it, the overlap will be visible as a thicker paint line. Paint one entire wall before taking a break.
7. Keep the roller on the wall. Spinning the roller in the air throws a fine mist of paint droplets across the room.

## How Many Coats?

With high-quality paint over a properly primed surface, two coats is standard. One coat rarely provides complete coverage—the wall color will show through subtly, especially along cut-in edges where the brush applies a thinner coat than the roller. Dark colors over light or light over dark may require three coats regardless of paint quality. Let each coat dry to the touch (typically 2–4 hours for latex paint) before applying the next. Re-coating too soon causes the roller to pick up the partially dry first coat, creating a textured, uneven surface.

## Paint Selection

**Sheen (finish level):**
- **Flat/Matte:** Hides wall imperfections best. No light reflection. Least durable. Use for ceilings, low-traffic rooms, adult bedrooms.
- **Eggshell:** Slight sheen, more washable than flat. Standard for living rooms, dining rooms, adult bedrooms.
- **Satin:** Noticeable sheen, scrubbable. Use for kitchens, bathrooms, kids' rooms, hallways, trim.
- **Semi-gloss:** Reflective, very durable, moisture-resistant. Use for trim, doors, cabinets, bathrooms.
- **High-gloss:** Mirror-like. Use for doors, cabinets, furniture. Shows every imperfection—walls must be flawless.

**Paint quality tiers:**
- Contractor grade ($20–25/gallon): More water, less solids. Requires 3+ coats. Used by landlords and flippers.
- Mid-grade ($35–45/gallon): Good coverage in 2 coats. Benjamin Moore Ben, Sherwin-Williams SuperPaint.
- Premium ($55–70/gallon): One-coat coverage in many situations, best durability. Benjamin Moore Aura, Sherwin-Williams Emerald.

The price difference between mid-grade and contractor grade per room is about $15–20. The extra coat of paint required by contractor grade costs more in time than the savings.

## Cleanup

- Clean brushes and rollers immediately after the final coat. Latex paint washes out with warm water and a small amount of dish soap. Work the bristles with your fingers until the water runs clear.
- Spin the brush between your palms to fling out excess water, reshape the bristles, and store hanging or in the original packaging sleeve to maintain shape.
- If you are painting the next day, wrap brushes and rollers tightly in plastic wrap or a plastic bag and store in the refrigerator. They will stay wet overnight and you can resume immediately without cleaning.
- Pour leftover paint from the tray back into the can. Close the lid tightly—place a cloth over the lid before hammering to prevent paint splatter.
