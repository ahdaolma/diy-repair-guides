## Annual Deck Inspection
![Replacing a damaged deck board](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.DcL7BtxZNb9tDJBGcGVJzgHaHa%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=9035b2dfa7133b73d720e12321f3633914a0d037b7bf797b0850ba373e189ff6&ipo=images)

The North American Deck and Railing Association recommends a thorough deck inspection at least once per year. Decks are exposed to sun, rain, snow, and freeze-thaw cycles—conditions that degrade wood and fasteners continuously. The three most common deck problems, in order of frequency: loose fasteners (nails or screws popping up), surface weathering (splintering, cracking, graying), and rot (typically at board ends, post bases, and ledger board connections).

A deck that is structurally compromised—particularly at the ledger board (where the deck attaches to the house)—is a collapse hazard. The Consumer Product Safety Commission estimates there are approximately 6,500 deck-related injuries requiring emergency room visits annually in the United States. Most are from structural failures that annual inspection would have identified.

## Fix 1: Loose or Popped Fasteners
![Sanding deck boards before staining](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.explicit.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.KxY58KPznBC3bfMex7mbFQHaE8%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=bc522ec6894de8fff89b40a707c46dad1b4a9220aeee576b54694df9b70f35b2&ipo=images)

Nails pop up from deck boards through repeated expansion and contraction cycles. A popped nail is a trip hazard and a sign that the nail has lost its grip.

1. Never hammer a popped nail back down—the hole is wallowed out and the nail will pop again within weeks.
2. Remove the nail completely with a cat's paw or hammer claw.
3. Replace with a deck screw (coated, exterior-rated, 2½–3 inches long). Deck screws have aggressive threads that bite into the joist and will not back out. Drive the screw into the same hole.
4. If the screw does not bite (the wood is too degraded), drill out the hole to ⅜ inch, glue in a hardwood dowel with exterior wood glue, cut it flush, and drive the screw into the dowel.

## Fix 2: Replace a Damaged Board
![Staining a repaired deck with roller](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.jW0lrNMCs5hGfjesYz5axQHaEK%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=08a79a6a929e870149a275e972b5c3a7feefa99793035d31b40dd46fc946f92b&ipo=images)

Boards that are severely cracked, splintered, or rotted must be replaced. Replacing a few boards is far cheaper than redecking.

1. Remove the damaged board: unscrew or cut the fasteners. If screws are stripped, use a screw extractor. If nails will not pull, cut them flush with a reciprocating saw (metal blade) slipped between the board and the joist.
2. Cut the replacement board to length. Pressure-treated lumber matches the original material. Cedar and redwood are more expensive but naturally rot-resistant. Composite decking (Trex, TimberTech) does not rot or splinter but costs 2–3x more and may not match existing wood boards in color.
3. Position the new board. Maintain a gap of approximately ⅛ to ¼ inch between boards for drainage and expansion. Pressure-treated wood shrinks as it dries—a board installed tight in May will have a ¼-inch gap by September.
4. Fasten with two deck screws per joist. For a clean, fastener-free surface, use hidden deck fasteners (camo system, deck clips) that screw in at an angle through the board edge.
5. Let pressure-treated wood weather for 3–6 months before staining. New pressure-treated lumber is saturated with treatment chemicals and will not absorb stain. Test by sprinkling water on the surface—if it beads up, the wood is not ready. If it soaks in within a few seconds, it is ready to stain.

## Fix 3: Address Rot

Rot begins where water accumulates: board ends, around fasteners, at post bases, and at the deck-to-house ledger connection.

**Board rot (ends and around fasteners):** Replace the board. Epoxy wood consolidant can stabilize small areas of surface rot but is a temporary fix—the rot organisms are already inside the wood fibers.

**Post base rot:** If a support post is rotted at ground level, the post must be replaced or the rot must be cut out and a new post section spliced in. Posts embedded in concrete or soil are particularly vulnerable—replace them with posts set on galvanized post bases elevated above the concrete or soil by at least 1 inch.

**Ledger board rot:** This is the most dangerous rot location. The ledger attaches the deck to the house. A rotted ledger can cause the deck to pull away from the house without warning. If the ledger shows any signs of rot, dark staining, or soft wood, stop using the deck and have a professional evaluate it. Ledger replacement requires proper flashing, lag screw or through-bolt attachment to the house framing, and often permits.

## Fix 4: Sand and Stain

1. Sweep the deck clean.
2. Sand the entire deck surface with a rented floor sander or orbital deck sander ($40–60/day). Use 60–80 grit. The goal is to remove the gray, weathered surface layer and expose fresh wood. Do not skip grits—starting with too fine a grit just polishes the gray layer.
3. Clean the deck thoroughly with a deck cleaner (oxygen bleach, not chlorine bleach—chlorine damages wood fibers). Apply with a pump sprayer, scrub with a stiff brush, rinse with a garden hose. Do not pressure-wash a wood deck—the high-pressure water erodes the soft springwood between the grain lines, leaving a permanently rough, splintery surface.
4. Let the deck dry for 48 hours of dry weather.
5. Apply stain with a roller and brush. Use an exterior penetrating stain (oil-based or water-based) formulated for decks—not paint, not film-forming solid stain. Penetrating stain soaks into the wood and does not peel. Film-forming products peel.
6. Apply two thin coats, not one thick coat. A thick coat forms a film that peels within a year.
7. Re-stain every 2–3 years depending on sun exposure and climate.
