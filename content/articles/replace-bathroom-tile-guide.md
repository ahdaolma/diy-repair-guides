## Assess Damage Before Removing Anything
![Removing a damaged bathroom wall tile](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.L3EwMmu_l76ZuHlgYi6fAAHaKr%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=46910b8afdbb5f6654b918fa4e3346cd87a809a197dfa9281c7688db5378b335&ipo=images)

A cracked bathroom tile is not just cosmetic—the crack allows water to reach the substrate behind the tile. In a shower or tub surround, a cracked tile is a water damage pathway. Replace it promptly.

Before removing the tile, determine whether the substrate is damaged:
- Tap the tile and the surrounding tiles with a screwdriver handle. A hollow sound means the tile has debonded from the substrate—moisture or installation failure.
- Push gently on the tile. If it moves, the substrate may be soft (water-damaged drywall or cement board).
- For shower walls: if multiple adjacent tiles sound hollow or the wall feels soft when pressed, the damage extends beyond one tile. You may need to remove multiple tiles and possibly replace the substrate—a much larger project.

## Tools and Materials

- Grout saw or oscillating multi-tool with carbide blade
- Cold chisel, hammer
- Notched trowel (¼ × ¼ inch)
- Thinset mortar (modified, for wet areas)
- Replacement tile(s)
- Matching grout (sanded or unsanded)
- Grout float, sponge, bucket
- Safety glasses

## Step 1: Remove the Damaged Tile
![Applying thinset for new bathroom tile](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.KVdMNi-EOQM1qUgmI6638QHaEK%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=be677aef1e247be7868c80204db044d5de9d39473d26803e54f4d76fd0fdfeaa&ipo=images)

1. Protect surrounding tiles by taping their edges with painter's tape.
2. Remove the grout around the damaged tile to full depth using a grout saw or oscillating tool with carbide blade. You must sever the grout bond on all four sides.
3. Drill several relief holes through the tile with a ¼-inch masonry bit. This weakens the tile so it breaks into controlled pieces rather than cracking unpredictably and damaging adjacent tiles.
4. Place a cold chisel in the center of the tile at a low angle. Tap with a hammer until the tile cracks. Remove the broken pieces from the center outward.
5. Chisel off the old thinset from the substrate. The surface must be flat and clean.
6. Inspect the substrate. If it is drywall (green board) and appears water-damaged (dark, soft, crumbling), the wall needs to be opened and the damaged section replaced. Do not install new tile over water-damaged substrate. If it is cement board and is intact, proceed.

## Step 2: Install the Replacement Tile
![Grouting new bathroom tile installation](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.KZPcpZLI3iIbmuy0hm8OCAHaHa%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=db0ac6b64a4b8b9b6e57463217375a577d6ab3773ffc7762c6279c589f9777e4&ipo=images)

1. Dry-fit the replacement tile. It should sit flush with surrounding tiles. If the existing thinset is too thick (common on older installations), you may need to chip more out or back-butter the tile more heavily.
2. Mix modified thinset mortar (modified = polymer additives for wet areas). Standard unmodified thinset is not rated for wet areas like showers. Consistency should be creamy peanut butter.
3. Comb thinset onto the substrate with the notched trowel. Back-butter the tile with a thin flat coat.
4. Press the tile into place. Wiggle slightly perpendicular to the trowel ridges to collapse them. Use spacers to maintain even grout joints.
5. Check for level with surrounding tiles using a straightedge. Adjust immediately if the tile is proud or recessed.
6. Let the thinset cure for 24 hours.

## Step 3: Grout and Seal

1. Remove spacers.
2. Mix grout (match color—bring a chip of old grout to the store). For joints ⅛ inch and under, use unsanded grout. For wider joints, use sanded.
3. Force grout into joints with a float, working diagonally.
4. Sponge off excess after 15–30 minutes with a damp sponge.
5. Buff haze with dry cloth after 2–3 hours.
6. Cure for 72 hours, then seal the grout with penetrating sealer.

## If You Cannot Find Matching Tile

- Check attics, basements, and under bathroom vanities for leftover tiles from the original installation.
- Remove a tile from an inconspicuous location (inside a closet, behind a mirror, inside a vanity) and use it to replace the damaged visible tile. Install the closest-match new tile in the hidden spot.
- As a last resort, replace the damaged tile with a decorative accent tile (different color or pattern, intentionally contrasting) and frame it as an intentional design feature.
