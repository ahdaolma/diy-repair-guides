## Turn Off the Water and Test It

![Faucet repair tools on a workbench](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.WEByqLo2DQBGfHEXmBkIqwHaHa%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=16467d8a301f3e2f4049c9e0fd013f5d81cf6f346aa2e7d9c712e71a27e628ea&ipo=images)

Before you pick up a screwdriver, locate the shutoff valves under the sink and turn both clockwise until they stop. Then open the faucet to verify water has stopped flowing. If the valves are stuck or corroded, do not force them—you risk snapping the valve body and flooding the cabinet. Instead, shut off the main water supply to the house. Find your main shutoff (usually in the basement, crawlspace, or near the water meter) and turn it clockwise. Open the lowest faucet in the house (basement sink or outdoor spigot) to drain the lines before you start work.

## Cover the Drain Immediately

Small screws, washers, clips, and springs have an uncanny ability to find open drains. Before disassembling anything, close the sink stopper or lay a rag over the drain opening. If you do drop a part down the drain despite this, do not run water—use a wet/dry vacuum or remove the P-trap underneath the sink (place a bucket under it first). Running water sends the part into the main drain, where retrieval becomes a plumber-level problem.

## Take the Old Part to the Store

![Replacement faucet cartridge and parts](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.alsK1TjD-QnXIsjVelLa2wHaHa%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=c4a3ddd1a77b66631aa22db016fdd2f5837428340e8d848439bef9f7d44a49f9&ipo=images)

Never guess the size of a washer, O-ring, or cartridge from memory. Washers alone come in over 30 sizes. Remove the worn part, put it in a plastic bag, and take it with you to the hardware store. While you are there, take photos of your faucet from multiple angles—especially any brand markings on the base or handle. If the plumbing aisle associate cannot identify the part, the photos may help. Better to spend 10 minutes at the store matching parts than to buy the wrong size, install it, discover it still drips, and do the entire job twice.

## Use Plumber's Grease, Not Petroleum Jelly

![Faucet disassembly showing internal components](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.k3XVBk7NViUhzoebyR6-WAHaE8%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=f0b240e6426aede4ffcb56d6bad1438970527aad176f230c227535f0dbc04b4a&ipo=images)

Coat new O-rings and rubber washers with silicone-based plumber's grease before installation. This lubricates the parts during assembly, prevents the rubber from binding and tearing as you tighten, and extends the life of the seals. Never use petroleum-based grease (Vaseline, axle grease, WD-40) on rubber plumbing parts—petroleum degrades natural rubber and most synthetic rubber compounds, causing them to swell, soften, and fail within weeks. A 0.5-ounce container of plumber's grease costs about $4 and lasts for dozens of repairs.

## Do Not Overtighten Anything

The number one DIY plumbing mistake is overtightening. Faucet stems, packing nuts, and mounting nuts are made of brass, which is soft. A quarter-turn past hand-tight is usually sufficient. Overtightening crushes washers and O-rings (causing immediate leaks), strips brass threads, and can crack the faucet body. If the faucet drips after reassembly, tighten very slightly—one-eighth turn at a time—until the drip stops. If you have to crank it down to stop the leak, the washer or seat is the wrong size or not seated correctly. Disassemble and inspect.

## Check the Valve Seat

If you replace the washer and the faucet still drips, the problem is the valve seat—the brass ring inside the faucet body that the washer presses against. Over years, water erosion creates grooves and pits in the seat. A new washer cannot form a seal against a rough surface. Run your fingertip over the seat. If it is not perfectly smooth, use a seat-dressing tool ($10–15 at any hardware store). Insert the tool into the faucet body, turn it by hand 3–5 rotations, and inspect. Repeat until the seat surface is smooth and shiny. This step adds 5 minutes to the repair and is the difference between a fix that lasts 6 months and one that lasts 10 years.

## Know When the Cartridge Is Stuck—and How to Free It

Cartridge faucets (Moen, Delta, Kohler) often seize in place from mineral buildup. If you pull on the stem and the cartridge does not budge, do not twist aggressively—you will snap the plastic stem off in the faucet body. Instead: soak the cartridge area with white vinegar for 30 minutes (vinegar dissolves calcium and lime deposits), then try again. If still stuck, use a cartridge puller tool ($12–20, brand-specific). The tool threads onto the cartridge stem and uses a screw mechanism to apply steady, straight-line extraction force. This is how plumbers do it without breaking anything.

## Check the Manufacturer Warranty Before You Buy

Moen, Delta, Kohler, Pfister, and American Standard all offer lifetime warranties on their faucet cartridges and finishes. Before buying a $25–$40 replacement cartridge, call the manufacturer's customer service line with your faucet model number. In many cases, they will ship a free replacement cartridge to your door within a week. You will need the model number (often on a tag under the sink or on the original packaging). Even without the model number, a clear photo of the faucet often suffices for warranty identification. This is not widely advertised, but it is official company policy—take advantage of it.
