## Wash the Walls Before You Paint

![Painter cutting in along trim with angled brush](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.iKIIKDfATJZWHX7Tb0hELAHaJg%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=7165050710a262d16b9e6b19a14a060b74d012bdc5a1a3fb6379f8833d3d73e3&ipo=images)

Paint does not bond to grease, dust, or skin oil. Kitchen walls accumulate a film of aerosolized cooking oil. Bathroom walls collect soap residue and hair product. Even bedroom walls build up dust and skin contact oils around light switches and headboard areas. Wash every wall with a TSP (trisodium phosphate) solution—½ cup per 2 gallons of warm water. A sponge mop makes washing an 8-foot wall fast with no ladder. Rinse with clean water and let dry completely. The 30 minutes this takes is the difference between paint that adheres for 10 years and paint that peels in 2.

## Buy the Most Expensive Brush You Can Afford

![Loading a paint roller correctly](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.vSxQLteIJZ3tBks85BykuAHaHa%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=a58071c9ded3dddf2d3c04d12e3a8fa2b3a9711b0346ab8191a60e0b1958f3b8&ipo=images)

A $15–18 Purdy or Wooster 2.5-inch angled sash brush holds more paint, releases it more evenly, and cuts a sharper line than a $4–6 hardware-store brush. The difference is in the bristle quality: Chinex (synthetic) bristles are tapered to a fine tip and flagged (split) at the ends, which holds paint in the brush rather than dripping it on the floor. A good brush, properly cleaned, lasts through dozens of rooms. A cheap brush sheds bristles into the paint, leaves brush marks, and makes cutting in—already the hardest skill—even harder. If you only upgrade one tool, make it the brush.

## Cut In One Wall at a Time

![Applying painters tape for sharp paint lines](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.J2jqsB0q-M-yY1J2ts2MvAAAAA%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=08d118d1fa6600f3bfcaab8fd011541197d345687e815393b7d44d9c00082fbe&ipo=images)

Do not cut in the entire room and then roll. By the time you finish cutting in the last wall, the cut-in band on the first wall has dried. When you roll over dried cut-in paint, the overlap creates a visible texture difference called a hat band or picture-frame effect. Instead, cut in one wall, immediately roll that wall, then move to the next wall. The cut-in band must be wet when the roller hits it so the textures blend seamlessly. If you are working alone, this means stopping between walls to clean your brush—or use two brushes and alternate.

## Back-Roll Every Section in One Direction

After applying paint with the W or N pattern and filling in, do not stop there. Lightly roll over the entire section one final time with parallel, floor-to-ceiling strokes, all in the same direction, using an almost-dry roller (no new paint loaded). This "laying off" aligns the paint stipple uniformly. Without it, random roller marks dry in different orientations and catch light differently, making the wall look patchy from certain angles. This final pass takes 10 seconds per section and eliminates the most common DIY painting tell.

## Use Canvas Drop Cloths, Not Plastic

Plastic drop cloths are slippery, tear easily, and paint drips sit on the surface—step in a drip and you track paint across the floor. Canvas drop cloths absorb drips, stay in place without tape, and are reusable for years. A 9×12 foot canvas drop cloth costs $15–25 and covers most of a standard bedroom floor. Fold it to fit against baseboards. If a spill soaks through canvas, it will stain the floor beneath—for carpeted rooms, layer plastic underneath canvas, or use a waterproof canvas-backed drop cloth.

## Pull Tape While the Paint Is Wet

The cleanest paint line comes from removing painter's tape while the final coat is still wet—within 30–60 minutes of application. If paint dries on the tape, it forms a film that bridges the tape and the wall. Pulling dried tape tears this film unevenly, leaving a ragged edge that can also peel the new paint off the wall. Remove tape by pulling it back on itself at a 45-degree angle, slowly and steadily. If you must leave tape on overnight, score the paint-tape edge with a utility knife before pulling to break the paint film.

## Pour Paint Through a Strainer

Lumps, dried paint skins, and debris in the paint can leave streaks and bumps on your wall. A disposable cone paint strainer costs $1 at any paint store. Pour every gallon through a strainer into the roller tray. This also catches any dried bits that fell into the can since the last time you used it. One speck of dried paint dragged under a roller leaves a 3-foot streak across a freshly painted wall.

## Do Not Skimp on Ceiling Paint

If you are painting the ceiling, use ceiling-specific paint (flat white, formulated to minimize splatter and hide imperfections). Do not use wall paint on the ceiling—wall paint formulas are thinner and drip more. Ceiling paint is thicker and "stays put." Also, paint the ceiling before the walls. It is nearly impossible to cut in a ceiling without getting paint on the wall. When you paint the walls second, you cover any ceiling paint that got on the walls with a clean cut line.

## Store the Roller in the Fridge Overnight

If you cannot finish in one day, do not wash the roller. Wrap it tightly in plastic wrap or slide it into a plastic grocery bag, squeeze out the air, and put it in the refrigerator. The cold temperature slows paint drying, and the sealed bag prevents a skin from forming. The roller will be ready to use the next morning. A roller stored this way stays usable for 24–36 hours. For brushes, the same method works. At the end of the project, clean everything thoroughly—a good brush that costs $18 should last through 15–20 rooms if properly maintained.
