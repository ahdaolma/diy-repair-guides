## Use White Lithium Grease, Not WD-40, for a Permanent Fix

WD-40 is a solvent and water displacer, not a long-term lubricant. It quiets a squeak for a few weeks, then evaporates. For a permanent fix, remove the hinge pin (tap upward from the bottom with a nail set), clean it with steel wool, and coat it lightly with white lithium grease. Reinsert and the squeak is gone for years. White lithium grease costs $5 for a tube that will service every hinge in the house.

## The Toothpick Trick Works—Here Is How to Do It Right

When a hinge screw spins in a wallowed-out hole: remove the screw, coat 2–3 wooden toothpicks in carpenter's glue, push them into the hole, break them off flush, wait 15 minutes, and reinstall the screw. Do not skip the glue—dry toothpicks will eventually loosen. Do not use more than 3 toothpicks in a standard #9 screw hole or the screw will not start. For holes so enlarged that toothpicks fall through, drill to ¼ inch and glue in a hardwood dowel. Fill, dry, drill a pilot hole, and screw. This costs $2 and fixes what would otherwise require replacing the entire jamb.

## Shim With Cardboard, Not Plastic

A cereal box is approximately 1/32 inch thick—the perfect shim material for adjusting door alignment. Cut cardboard to match the hinge leaf shape, place between the hinge and the jamb, and reinstall the screws through it. One layer corrects a 1/16-inch gap change at the door edge. Stack 2–3 layers as needed. Do not use plastic (credit cards, milk jugs)—it compresses over time and the screws will loosen. Cardboard compresses slightly but holds its thickness under screw compression.

## One 3-Inch Screw Can Pull a Sagging Door Back Into Alignment

Replace one short hinge screw in the top hinge (on the jamb side) with a 3-inch construction screw of the same gauge. Drive it through the jamb into the stud behind it. Tighten until you see the door gap equalize—stop before the screw head strips or the jamb bends. This pulls the top hinge closer to the stud, lifting the latch side of the door. This is the most effective single trick for a sagging door and costs $0.10 in screws.

## Work on One Hinge at a Time

When removing hinge screws for any repair, never remove all three hinges simultaneously. The door is heavy—a solid-core interior door weighs 60–80 pounds, an exterior door 100+. Work on one hinge at a time, leaving at least two hinges attached to support the door. If you must remove all hinges, have a helper support the door or drive a wedge under it. A door that falls pulls the remaining hinge screws out of the jamb at an angle, enlarging the holes and creating a much bigger problem than the one you started with.
