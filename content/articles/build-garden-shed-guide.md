## Why Build Instead of Buy: The Cost Math

![DIY garden shed step by step](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.nvDCKoo74JRHu1vhiIqIqgHaHy%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=581d0929224d384e5e30942b6a5977e06437bc5960b9804d5b81168e471ca735&ipo=images)

A pre-built 8x10 wood shed delivered and installed costs $3,500-$6,000. A resin/plastic shed of the same size costs $1,500-$2,500 but has the structural integrity of a Rubbermaid bin and a lifespan of 7-12 years before UV degradation makes the plastic brittle. DIY wood construction: $1,500-$2,500 in materials, 3-5 weekends of work, and you get a custom-sized shed built to YOUR specifications: exact dimensions, window and door placement, shelving, and a lifespan of 20-30 years with proper maintenance.

This guide covers an 8-foot by 10-foot shed — the largest size that typically does not require a building permit in most municipalities (check locally — 120 sq ft is a common cutoff). Gable roof (the classic triangular A-frame). Asphalt shingles to match your house. Pressure-treated floor on concrete pier blocks. This design has been built by thousands of DIYers and refined over decades of shed-building forums.

**Total time estimate:** 3-5 weekends (40-60 hours) for one person with occasional help for roof sheathing. Two people can complete it in 3 weekends.

## Before You Build: Permits, Codes, and Property Lines

![Shed foundation and framing plan](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.34IPDJGVUCaZAVsBf8o4PwHaHa%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=d1e9b84d7f5ba18103194f1115654321f659fbf549dce8ce50829d6ac0dd1be6&ipo=images)

**Call 811 before digging.** This is free. They mark underground utilities within 48-72 hours. Hitting a gas line or electrical conduit with a post hole digger turns a shed project into an emergency response. 811 is the law in every US state — not optional.

**Check setback requirements.** Most municipalities require sheds to be 3-5 feet from property lines and 10+ feet from the main structure. Your local building department website has this information, usually under "accessory structures." Get it in writing or via email — a verbal confirmation from a clerk means nothing if a code enforcement officer tags you later.

**Building permit threshold:** Typically 120 sq ft (8x15, 10x12) or 200 sq ft depending on jurisdiction. An 8x10 = 80 sq ft — under most thresholds. But some HOAs and historic districts require approval for ANY structure. Check. A stop-work order mid-build is expensive and demoralizing.

## Foundation: Concrete Pier Blocks on Pressure-Treated Skids

The simplest code-compliant shed foundation. No concrete mixing. No digging below frost line. Pier blocks rest on the ground (or on 4 inches of compacted gravel for better drainage), and the shed floor frame sits on pressure-treated 4x4 skids that span across the pier blocks. The entire structure "floats" on the ground — acceptable for sheds under 120 sq ft in most jurisdictions.

**Steps:**
1. Clear and level a 12x14-foot area (the shed + 2 feet of clearance on all sides for working and maintenance). Remove sod. Lay landscape fabric ($15/roll) to prevent weed growth under the shed.
2. Spread 4 inches of 3/4-inch crushed gravel ($40/ton, 1.5 tons = $60) across the entire area. Compact with a hand tamper ($25) or rent a plate compactor ($50/4 hours). The gravel provides drainage — without it, water pools under the shed and rots the floor joists from below in 5-7 years.
3. Arrange 12 pier blocks in a 3x4 grid: 3 rows (along the 8-foot depth), 4 columns (along the 10-foot width). Blocks spaced 3-4 feet apart. Each block must be perfectly level. Use a 4-foot level ($15) and adjust with gravel underneath.
4. Lay three 10-foot 4x4 pressure-treated skids across the pier blocks (perpendicular to the floor joists — the skids run the 10-foot width, the joists will run the 8-foot depth). Check level across all three skids with an 8-foot straightedge. Shimming at this stage prevents every subsequent problem — an out-of-level foundation means out-of-square walls and a roof that won't sit flat.

## Floor: 2x6 Joists on 16-Inch Centers

![6x4 shed floor framing plan](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.CghlJsQz2xeqcQYcxVqNGwHaJz%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=d5cb6ee431e425c35871b9e075611e58a552323749065069ef3cc44ed72d2045&ipo=images)

The floor is a rectangular frame of 2x6 pressure-treated lumber, 8 feet wide by 10 feet long, with joists every 16 inches on center. The 2x6 dimension is important — 2x4 joists bounce when you walk on them. 2x6 is solid. Pressure-treated because the floor is inches from the ground and WILL encounter moisture.

**Steps:**
1. Build the outer rim: two 10-foot 2x6 rim joists and two 8-foot 2x6 end joists. The 8-foot pieces go INSIDE the 10-foot pieces to create an 8x10 rectangle (the 10-foot pieces extend the full length; the 8-foot pieces fit between them). Secure with three 16d nails at each corner.
2. Install joist hangers every 16 inches along the inside of the 10-foot rim joists. Use 1.5-inch hanger nails (NOT screws — they shear under load). This is structural code: joist hangers must be nailed with manufacturer-specified fasteners.
3. Insert 8-foot 2x6 joists into hangers. Nail each hanger to the joist with six 1.5-inch hanger nails (3 per side). The floor should now be a grid of 11 joists spanning 8 feet, supported by hangers on both ends.
4. Check square: measure corner-to-corner diagonals. They MUST be equal with 1/4-inch. If not, rack the frame (push one corner diagonally) until the diagonals match, then temporarily nail a diagonal brace across the frame to hold it square.
5. Apply a continuous bead of construction adhesive to the top of every joist. Lay 3/4-inch tongue-and-groove plywood subfloor perpendicular to the joists (the 8-foot edge runs across the joists). Stagger the sheets so four corners never meet at one point. Screw every 6 inches along joists with 2-inch deck screws. The adhesive + screws create a composite floor that won't squeak — skip the adhesive and you'll regret it within 2 years.

## Walls: Standard 2x4 Framing with Sheathing

**Wall framing (one weekend):**
1. Build four wall sections flat on the subfloor, then tilt them up. This is standard construction — building walls lying down, squaring them, sheathing them, then standing them up. Each wall: a 2x4 bottom plate, a 2x4 top plate, and 2x4 studs every 16 inches on center.
2. **The 8-foot back wall:** One bottom plate, one top plate (both 10 feet long), 8-foot studs between them. Total height: 8 feet + 3 inches (two plates) = 8 feet 3 inches.
3. **The 8-foot front wall:** Same, but door rough opening: 34 inches wide (32-inch door + 2 inches for the door frame) by 82 inches tall (80-inch door + 2 inches). The rough opening has a header: two 2x6s nailed together with 1/2-inch plywood sandwiched between, standing on edge, supported by jack studs (trimmed studs that support the header) and king studs (full-height studs on either side of the jack studs). The header transfers the roof load around the door opening.
4. **Gable end walls** (two identical 8-foot-wide walls): These fit between the front and back walls and will later have the gable (triangular roof peak) built on top. Standard stud framing. Frame window rough openings (26x38 inches for 24x36 windows) — header, jack studs, king studs, sill.
5. Sheath each wall with 1/2-inch CDX plywood while it's still lying flat. Nail every 6 inches at edges, 12 inches in the field with 8d nails. The sheathing makes each wall a rigid panel that won't rack.
6. Tilt up the back wall first (heaviest — get help). Brace it plumb with 2x4s temporarily nailed to the wall and staked to the ground. Tilt the side walls, brace. Tilt the front wall (door opening needs a temporary 2x4 brace across it to prevent racking until the door is installed). Nail the walls together at corners with 16d nails every 12 inches.
7. Wrap the sheathed walls in house wrap (Tyvek), stapled every 12 inches. Overlap horizontal seams 6 inches. Overlap vertical seams 12 inches. Tape all seams with Tyvek tape ($10/roll). The house wrap is the water-resistive barrier — it stops bulk water that gets behind the siding from reaching the sheathing. Siding sheds 95% of water; house wrap catches the 5% that gets through.

## Roof: Gable Trusses or Stick-Framed Rafters

![Gable roof sheathing](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.8OdYaNeSQmQhR1klKd_LWAAAAA%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=92233aacce15e9c5de96149d6688eecb564563e84c81edb5e6af91549a0754bc&ipo=images)

For a small shed, stick-framing individual rafters is straightforward. A 6:12 pitch (the roof rises 6 inches for every 12 inches of horizontal run) sheds snow, provides headroom, and is easy to frame.

**Steps:**
1. The top plate of the side walls serves as the rafter plate. Lay out rafter positions at 16 inches on center along both plates.
2. Cut rafters from 2x6 lumber using a rafter square (Speed Square, $10). The rafter has a bird's mouth notch where it sits on the wall plate and an angled cut at the peak where pairs of rafters meet at the ridge board. Use a rafter table (printed on the Speed Square) or an online rafter calculator. For an 8-foot span with 6:12 pitch: rafter length approximately 5 feet 4 inches from ridge to wall.
3. Install a 2x8 ridge board spanning the 10-foot length, supported temporarily by 2x4s on each end. The ridge board sits at the peak, and rafters are nailed to it in opposing pairs.
4. Nail rafters to the ridge board (three 16d nails through each rafter into the ridge) and to the wall plate (toenail with two 16d nails on each side).
5. Install collar ties: 2x4s nailed horizontally across opposing rafters in the upper third of the rafter length, every 4 feet. These prevent the roof from spreading under load (snow, wind). Code requirement for most jurisdictions.
6. Sheath the roof with 1/2-inch OSB, staggering sheets. Nail every 6 inches at edges, 12 inches in the field. Leave a 1/8-inch gap between sheets for expansion.
7. Install drip edge along the eaves (bottom edge) first, then along the rakes (angled sides). The drip edge directs water into the gutters and off the fascia, preventing water from wicking back under the shingles.
8. Roll #15 roofing felt horizontally, starting at the bottom eave and working upward, overlapping each course by 4 inches. Staple in place.
9. Shingle: Start with a starter strip (cut the tabs off regular shingles or buy a starter roll, $25). Install shingles from the bottom up, overlapping according to the exposure line printed on the shingle (typically 5 inches). Nail each shingle with four 1.25-inch roofing nails — two on each side, 1 inch from the edge and above the sealant strip. Cap the ridge with ridge cap shingles bedded in roofing cement.
10. The gable ends get vertical siding cut to match the roofline angle or vented gable vents ($15 each for 12x18 aluminum louver vents) for attic ventilation.

## Interior and Finishing

Install the pre-hung exterior door in the rough opening. Shim the door frame plumb and level (essential for the door to swing properly and latch). Fill gaps between the frame and rough opening with expanding foam (low-expansion "window and door" formula, not regular Great Stuff which expands with enough force to warp a door frame).

Install windows with flashing tape at the sill, then the sides, then the top — shingle-style so water drains outward at every layer.

Siding goes on last. LP SmartSide is a wood-strand engineered siding that looks like wood but resists rot and insects for 50 years (warrantied). Prime and paint all cut edges before installation — this is the difference between siding that lasts 50 years and siding that rots from the edges in 15.

Paint or stain with exterior-grade product. Two coats. Use a paint sprayer ($40 rental) for speed; brush and roller for precision. The paint is not just cosmetic — it's the UV and moisture barrier that makes the siding, trim, and door last decades instead of years.

## When to Hire a Pro

Building a shed is a 40-60 hour project for a first-timer. If you have no experience with framing, no tools (buying everything new adds $500-$800), no help for the heavy lifts (walls, roof sheathing), or a sloped site that requires a retaining wall or poured concrete foundation, the cost savings over a pre-built shed shrink significantly. A delivered and installed 8x10 shed at $3,500 = $3,500. A DIY shed at $2,800 materials + $700 tools + 50 hours of your labor = $3,500 plus 6 weekends. The DIY value is customization and pride, not just money.
