## Find the Joist With a Stud Finder, Not Guesswork

![Floor joist visible from basement below](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.CFYkUSU0OPqyi0gKQuvUwwHaE9%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=8242a6c3c383fa506984f50fb2dfe038441be6f3775ec4180e38937724e01beb&ipo=images)

Driving a breakaway screw into empty space between joists does nothing. Use a stud finder with "deep scan" mode to locate joists through carpet and pad. Verify by finding a floor vent—lift the vent cover and look inside for a visible joist edge. Joists are typically spaced 16 inches on center (sometimes 24 inches in newer construction). Measure from the confirmed joist to your squeak in 16-inch increments to confirm the layout. Mark both edges of the joist with painter's tape—the screw needs to hit the center of the 1½-inch-wide joist.

## Use the Squeak No More Kit on Carpet

![Breakaway screw being driven through carpet](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.QKnGPvBE1G8xr9r11SXKMgHaHa%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=c86a964197609f2cbae5058e23a2d3676674194a9ddeedaf70c2bbf7bca2598f&ipo=images)

The Squeak No More kit ($20–25 at hardware stores or online) is the best solution for carpeted floors with no below access. The scored screws are designed to snap at exactly the right depth—below the carpet surface but anchored into the joist. The tripod alignment fixture holds the screw at the correct angle. Drive screws about every 6–8 inches along the squeaky joist. The broken screw heads are invisible under the carpet. One kit includes 50 screws and covers roughly 25 linear feet of joist.

## Shim From Below When You Have Access

![Wood shim being inserted between subfloor and joist](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.qRjSYqtSS4dFt5BAjr7tywHaF0%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=21a1de8bb85626dbac6020b4754209021a381d57352cab97337b6afbd501c424&ipo=images)

If you can access the floor from below (unfinished basement, drop ceiling), shimming is the simplest permanent fix. Have someone walk on the squeak from above while you watch. You will see a hairline gap open between the subfloor and the joist. Insert a thin cedar shingle coated with carpenter's glue (Titebond III) into the gap. Tap gently—just enough to fill the gap. Over-shimming lifts the finished floor. Let the glue cure 24 hours. For larger gaps, skip shims and drive 1¼-inch screws at an angle through the subfloor into the joist.

## Try Talcum Powder Before Drilling Holes

For high-pitched squeaks between adjacent floorboards (not deep structural groans), try talcum powder, graphite powder, or powdered soapstone. Sprinkle it liberally over the squeaky joint, work it in by walking over it, and wipe the excess. The powder lubricates the wood-on-wood friction. This is a temporary fix (6–12 months) but costs nothing, takes 30 seconds, and is completely non-invasive. If it works, you know the problem is board-to-board friction, not a loose subfloor. If it does not work, you have lost nothing and can proceed to mechanical methods.

## Never Re-Nail a Popped Floor Nail

If a nail has worked its way up and is protruding from the floor, do not hammer it back down. The hole is now wallowed out—the nail has zero holding power and will pop again within weeks. Remove the nail completely using a cat's paw or by gripping the head with pliers. Replace with a trim-head screw of the same or slightly larger diameter, driven into the same hole. The screw threads grip fresh wood and will not back out. Fill the screw head depression with wood filler and touch up with stain. One screw permanently fixes what a nail would fail at repeatedly.

## Pre-Drill Hardwood to Prevent Splitting

Old hardwood is dry and brittle. Driving a screw directly into it without a pilot hole will split the board along the grain, creating a worse problem than the squeak. Always drill a pilot hole—use a bit roughly the diameter of the screw shank minus the threads. For a #8 trim-head screw, a ⅛-inch pilot bit is appropriate. If you are unsure, drill a test hole in an inconspicuous area (inside a closet) to confirm the bit size does not split the wood before drilling in a visible area.
