## Three Common Door Hinge Problems
![Lubricating a squeaky door hinge pin](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.79hom_RvirdKzOXCZlMNNgHaHa%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=691d7dbeaf232f6a6c7428ef522d1f3349ec4902d7804499172247898597b641&ipo=images)

Door hinge problems fall into three categories, each with a different fix:

1. **Squeaking:** Metal-on-metal friction at the hinge pin. Fix: lubricate.
2. **Loose screws:** The screw holes in the wood are wallowed out and screws will not hold. Fix: fill the holes.
3. **Binding or sagging:** The door rubs the frame or will not latch. Fix: shim or adjust the hinges.

All three fixes use materials you likely already have and take under 15 minutes.

## Fix 1: Squeaky Hinges

1. Open the door and locate the hinge pins—the vertical metal rods running through each hinge knuckle.
2. Spray a small amount of penetrating lubricant (WD-40, silicone spray, or white lithium grease) at the top of each hinge pin where it enters the knuckle. A single short spray per hinge is sufficient.
3. Work the door back and forth several times to distribute the lubricant.
4. Wipe away any drips with a paper towel. Lubricant dripping down the door frame stains paint.
5. For stubborn squeaks: remove the hinge pin. Tap it upward from the bottom with a nail set and hammer. Clean the pin with steel wool to remove rust and old dried lubricant. Apply a light coat of white lithium grease to the pin, reinsert, and tap down. This is a permanent fix for all but severely rusted hinges.

**What not to use:** Do not use cooking oil, olive oil, or vegetable oil. They go rancid, gum up, and attract dust. Do not use graphite powder on door hinges—it is for locks, and the black powder stains painted surfaces.

## Fix 2: Loose Screws / Stripped Holes
![Repairing stripped screw hole with toothpicks and glue](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.sKUUU7SOH2uJZ-kdYKJGqAHaEK%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=27b5c32f51aca3c8c7442deb83f1f837feb5d4472acdad2df8d53c0bf2c39ae4&ipo=images)

When hinge screws spin freely without tightening, the wood fibers around the screw have been destroyed. The fix creates new material for the screw threads to bite into.

**Method 1: Toothpicks and wood glue (best for standard hinges):**
1. Remove the loose screw.
2. Dip 2–3 wooden toothpicks (or a wood matchstick with the head removed) in wood glue (Titebond or similar).
3. Push the glue-coated toothpicks into the screw hole as far as they will go. Break or cut them off flush with the surface.
4. Let the glue dry for 15–30 minutes.
5. Reinsert the screw. The toothpicks provide fresh wood for the threads to grip. Tighten firmly but do not overtighten—the toothpicks have more give than solid wood.

**Method 2: Wooden dowel (for heavily damaged holes):**
1. Drill out the stripped hole with a drill bit sized to match a hardwood dowel (typically ¼ or 5/16 inch).
2. Glue a piece of dowel into the hole.
3. Let the glue cure fully (1–2 hours).
4. Cut the dowel flush with the surface.
5. Drill a new pilot hole (slightly smaller than the screw) and reinsert the screw.

**Method 3: Longer screws (quick fix for hinge-to-jamb screws):**
Replace the standard ¾-inch or 1-inch hinge screws with 2½-inch or 3-inch screws of the same diameter. The longer screw reaches past the door jamb into the structural framing (the king stud or jack stud behind the jamb). This not only secures the hinge but can also pull a sagging door back into alignment. Use this method selectively—one long screw in each hinge is usually sufficient. Do not overtighten to the point of pulling the jamb out of position.

## Fix 3: Binding or Sagging Door
![Shimming door hinge to correct alignment](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.explicit.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.z881UKf3QgKrDyP2Q6X5BgHaD4%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=a3f14c0edc651a780eb96d658d87fa7a9e8702ac20afb53ed0e56ee6c6c17163&ipo=images)

If the door rubs the frame at the top or latch side, the hinge geometry has shifted. Diagnose by looking at the gap between the door and frame—it should be a consistent ⅛ inch all around. If the gap is wider at the bottom and zero at the top on the latch side, the top hinge has pulled away from the jamb.

**Fix: Shim behind the hinge leaf.**

1. Determine which hinge is causing the problem. If the door rubs at the top latch side, the top hinge needs shimming. If it rubs at the bottom, shim the bottom hinge.
2. Remove the screws from the hinge leaf on the jamb side (not the door side). Support the door—it will drop when the hinge is detached.
3. Cut a piece of thin cardboard (cereal box cardboard is approximately 1/32 inch thick) to the shape of the hinge leaf. You can stack multiple layers for more adjustment.
4. Place the cardboard shim between the hinge leaf and the jamb. Reattach the screws through the shim.
5. Test the door. If still rubbing, add another layer. If the gap on the other side is now too wide, remove a layer. Small adjustments—the thickness of a business card—make visible changes at the door edge.

**Alternative: Bend the hinge knuckles.** For a door rubbing at the top latch side, remove the hinge pin from the middle hinge, insert an adjustable wrench over one hinge knuckle, and bend it very slightly toward the jamb. Reinsert the pin. This adjusts the door angle without shimming. This method is fast but risks damaging the hinge finish. Use it as a last resort before hinge replacement.

## When to Replace the Hinges

Replace the hinges if: the knuckles are worn oblong (the pin wobbles visibly), the hinge leaves are bent or cracked, or the screw holes in the hinge itself are stripped. Standard interior door hinges (3½ inch with ¼-inch radius corners) cost $3–5 each. Match the finish (satin nickel, oil-rubbed bronze, polished brass) and the corner radius (¼-inch or 5/8-inch square). Bring an old hinge to the store for exact matching.
