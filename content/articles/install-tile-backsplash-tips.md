## Spend More Time on Layout Than on Tiling

![Planning a tile layout with laser level](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.ehigIWAvD9JSqsT8b8tW9gHaEK%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=b2ca2cf2db20c123cf4042ad14d8bd1c2aa5650d82b0977bf34d9e179e3bbf2b&ipo=images)

The single most important tip from professional tilers: spend at least 45 minutes on layout before you open the thinset. Dry-lay the entire first two rows across the whole wall. Measure the end cuts. Check for slivers. If an outlet will break a tile in a way that leaves a U-shaped sliver that cannot be cut reliably, shift the layout half a tile. Layout determines 80 percent of how the finished backsplash looks. The remaining 20 percent is execution. A perfectly tiled backsplash with a bad layout will always look wrong; a decently tiled backsplash with a good layout looks professional.

## Use a Laser Level, Not a Bubble Level

A 4-foot bubble level works, but a self-leveling cross-line laser is dramatically faster and more accurate. For about $40 to $80, a laser projects horizontal and vertical lines across the entire wall. You can see at a glance whether a tile is drifting, whether a row is staying straight, and whether the pattern is staying plumb over outlets and windows. If you tile more than one backsplash in your life, the laser pays for itself in time saved checking and rechecking. If you do not buy one, at minimum check level after every three tiles in the first two rows. Most drift starts in row one or two and compounds.

## Back-Butter Every Tile

![Applying thinset to the back of a tile](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.AHH7m4uBbc_c5vzua8uCGAHaEK%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=9652903ffe9935fc0119ef0f41eda4e1883a47c0409fed3c1c53caf341ab7f18&ipo=images)

Combing thinset onto the wall alone leaves the bond at the mercy of how well you pressed the tile in and whether the ridges collapsed fully. Back-buttering—spreading a thin, even coat of thinset on the back of every tile with the flat side of the trowel—eliminates hollow spots and guarantees full coverage. It adds about 5 seconds per tile. The Tile Council of North America requires 80 percent coverage in dry areas and 95 percent in wet areas. Back-buttering is the most reliable way to hit those numbers on a vertical surface.

## Mix Thinset in Small Batches

A full 25-pound bag of thinset mixed at once gives you roughly three to four hours of pot life. But the open time on the wall is only 15 to 30 minutes, and most first-time tilers cannot spread and cover an entire bag''s worth of thinset in that window. Mix half a bag at a time, or even a quarter-bag if you are working slowly. Thinset costs about $15 a bag; wasting half a bag because it skinned over is a $7.50 mistake. Pulling tile off the wall because it did not bond and redoing the work is a multi-hour mistake.

## Do Not Tile Over Wallpaper or Glossy Paint

Thinset bonds mechanically—it grips the surface texture, not the surface itself. Wallpaper, semi-gloss paint, and high-gloss paint provide too smooth a surface for mechanical bonding. If your wall has wallpaper, strip it completely, wash off residual paste with warm water and a sponge, and prime with a PVA drywall primer. If the wall has glossy paint, scuff it thoroughly with 80-grit sandpaper until the surface feels rough to the touch, then wipe with a damp cloth to remove dust. Skipping this step can result in tiles popping off the wall within a year—and they usually take chunks of drywall paper with them.

## Accept That Outlets Will Be the Hardest Part

The sections around electrical outlets consume roughly 30 percent of the total project time but only 5 percent of the square footage. Each outlet requires marking, cutting a notch (sometimes a U-shape or L-shape), test-fitting, and frequently recutting. Tile nippers are the right tool for small notch adjustments. Make the notch slightly larger than you think you need—the outlet ears and cover plate hide the gap, and a tile that cracks because you forced it over an outlet box is unfixable without removing and replacing it. Budget an extra 10 minutes per outlet if you have never done this before.

## Clean Grout Haze Within 24 Hours

Grout haze—that whitish film left on the tile surface after grouting—is easy to remove when fresh and extremely difficult once cured. After your final sponge pass, wait one hour and buff the tile surface with a dry microfiber cloth or a piece of cheesecloth. If you still see haze the next morning, use a grout haze remover (a mild acid cleaner, usually phosphoric acid-based, about $8 at any hardware store) and a scrub pad. Do not use vinegar; it is too weak and too easy to over-apply, which can etch natural stone tile and weaken grout.

## Grout the Inside Corners, Then Caulk Over Them

The joint where two walls meet in a corner is a movement joint. Wall framing expands and contracts seasonally, and the two walls move independently. Grout in these corners will crack—guaranteed, usually within six months to a year. The professional solution: grout the corner lightly for backing, let it cure, then apply color-matched silicone caulk over the grout. The grout fills the depth of the joint so you are not trying to bridge a 3/8-inch gap with caulk alone. The caulk provides the flexible surface that will not crack. If caulk alone bridges a wide gap, it sags, ripples, and eventually pulls away from one side.

## Do Not Rush the Sealer

Penetrating grout sealer works by soaking into the grout and filling the microscopic pores. If the grout is not fully cured (48 to 72 hours, depending on humidity and the specific grout), those pores still contain moisture and the sealer cannot penetrate. The result is a weak surface barrier that wears off in weeks instead of lasting one to two years. Wait the full curing time listed on the grout bag. If you are unsure whether the grout is cured, tape a small square of plastic wrap tightly over a grout line for 30 minutes. If condensation appears under the plastic, the grout is still releasing moisture. Wait another day and test again.

## Buy an Extra Box of Tile and Store It

Dye lots change between production runs. The same tile from the same manufacturer ordered six months later can arrive in a visibly different shade—slightly whiter, slightly creamier, or with a different level of gloss. If you crack a tile during installation or chip one a year later while moving a heavy pot, and you do not have a matching replacement, you will be pulling tiles off the wall and hoping the new box matches. One extra box costs $15 to $30 and stores easily in a basement or utility closet. Write the purchase date and store name on the box with a marker so you can reorder if needed, though you should not count on a perfect match even with that information.
