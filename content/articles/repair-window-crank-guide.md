## The Crank Spins But the Window Doesn't Move

![Replace casement window crank operator](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.P8xxn4WZC7NZlVZ5Iq10fAHaHa%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=7664ce95524120716a04efb428e9c6633a603b7ec2eb9e007ed013c26e59f4c7&ipo=images)

Casement windows (windows that crank open outward, hinged on one side) use a mechanical operator — a gear mechanism that converts rotary motion (turning the crank) into linear motion that pushes the window open. The operator is the most common failure point because the internal gears are made of die-cast zinc or pot metal. Over 10-15 years of use, the gear teeth wear down or strip completely. When the crank spins freely without the window moving, the operator gears have failed. When the crank is impossibly stiff, the operator or the hinge mechanism needs lubrication.

Replacement operators cost $15-$40 and are universal for most brands (Truth, Andersen, Pella, Marvin all use compatible mounting patterns — the industry converged on a standard in the 1980s). A window company will charge $150-$300 to replace an operator. DIY it for $20 in 20 minutes.

## Identify Your Operator Type

**Look at the crank handle attachment.** The handle attaches to the operator spindle (the metal shaft protruding from the operator housing) with a set screw. The shape of the spindle tells you the operator type:

- **Splined spindle (ridges/grooves):** Most common. Truth Encore, Truth Maxim, most generic operators. The handle slides onto the splined shaft and is secured with a set screw.
- **D-shaft (flat on one side):** Andersen, some Pella. The flat side aligns with a matching flat in the handle.
- **T-bar or cross-pin:** Older (pre-1990) casement windows. Replacement operators still available (Prime-Line H 3525, $18).

**Measure the arm length.** With the window fully closed, measure from the center of the operator spindle to the center of the pivot point where the arm connects to the window sash track. Standard lengths: 9 inches, 11 inches, or 13.5 inches. The replacement arm must match this length exactly — an arm that's too short won't fully open the window; an arm that's too long binds against the track.

## Replacement Procedure (20 Minutes)

**Materials:** Replacement operator ($15-$40), #2 Phillips screwdriver, flat-head screwdriver, silicone spray lubricant ($5).

1. **Remove the crank handle.** Locate the set screw on the underside of the handle where it meets the spindle. Loosen with a flat-head screwdriver or hex key (typically 1/8-inch Allen). The handle slides off the spindle. If stuck from years of paint/corrosion, apply penetrating oil (WD-40, $5) and wait 10 minutes.

2. **Remove the operator cover.** The operator is housed in a metal or plastic housing mounted to the windowsill or the bottom of the window frame. Pry off the snap-on plastic cover (if present) to expose the mounting screws.

3. **Disconnect the operator arm from the sash track.** The operator arm has a small guide shoe or roller that slides in a track on the bottom of the window sash. Lift the window sash slightly and slide the guide shoe out of the track. On some models, you need to slide it to the open position to reach the release point. If the window is stuck closed because the operator is broken, you may need to manually push the window open from outside while someone turns the crank — the stripped gears may catch just enough to move the arm.

4. **Remove the old operator.** Two to four Phillips screws hold the operator to the windowsill or frame. Remove them. The entire operator assembly lifts out.

5. **Install the new operator.** Position the new operator in the same orientation. The arm is usually pre-attached in the closed position. Screw it down. Do NOT overtighten — the operator housing is often plastic and can crack.

6. **Connect the arm to the sash track.** Slide the guide shoe back into the track. Test the mechanism by turning the crank HANDLE (reattach it temporarily) — the window should open and close smoothly.

7. **Lubricate.** Spray silicone lubricant ($5) on the operator gears (visible through the housing opening), the arm pivot points, and the sash track. Silicone lubricant, NOT WD-40 — WD-40 is a solvent, not a long-term lubricant. It washes away within weeks and leaves metal parts dry. Silicone spray (Blaster or 3-in-One brand) stays in place for years and won't attract dust.

8. **Reattach the handle.** Slide it onto the spindle, tighten the set screw firmly. Test full open and close 3-4 times.

## Finding the Right Replacement Part

**Universal operators:** Truth Encore ($20) and Prime-Line H 3526 ($18) fit most post-1980 casement windows. Home Depot and Lowe's stock these in the window hardware aisle. They come with both left-hand and right-hand configurations in the same package — you flip the arm orientation for LH vs RH windows.

**Brand-specific:** Andersen, Pella, and Marvin use proprietary operators for some models. Identify your window brand from the etching on the glass (look in the corner for the manufacturer logo and date code) or from a label on the window frame. Order from the manufacturer's parts website or from Swisco.com ($15-$40). You'll need the window's serial number (etched on the glass or on a sticker on the frame) for an exact match.

**If you don't know the brand:** Remove the old operator and bring it to a window supply store or Home Depot. Match it visually to the universal options. The bolt pattern (distance between the two mounting screws) is standardized at 5 inches center-to-center for most operators. If the bolt pattern matches and the arm length matches, the universal operator fits.

## When the Operator Isn't the Problem

- **Window is stiff to crank but the operator gears are fine:** The hinge mechanism (the scissor arms on the top and bottom of the window) is dirty or unlubricated. Clean with a wire brush, spray with silicone lubricant. Work the window open and closed 5-6 times to distribute the lubricant. If the hinges are rusted solid, replace the hinge set ($25-$50, brand-specific).
- **Window won't close flush against the frame:** The sash is misaligned on the hinges. Loosen the hinge mounting screws slightly, push the window into the correct closed position, retighten screws.
- **Crank handle keeps falling off:** The set screw is stripped or missing. Replace the handle ($8-$15 for universal handles). The handle fits any operator with a compatible spindle type — you don't need the original brand.
