## Why Shower Drains Clog
![Shower drain clogged with hair and soap scum](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.XinpFLc0KmKz7PUK0gZGFwHaIz%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=1840f2e9cd186eaf0edbe3892886a4998b258d4359741dfbcc2187e1e10f944d&ipo=images)

Shower drains clog for two reasons: hair and soap scum. Hair catches on the drain crossbars or stopper mechanism inside the pipe, forming a net that traps every subsequent strand. Soap scum—a waxy residue formed when soap reacts with minerals in hard water—coats the hair mass and the pipe walls, binding everything into a dense, slimy plug that restricts water flow. According to plumbing service data from Roto-Rooter, shower drain clogs account for roughly 22% of all residential drain calls, and the average shower drain accumulates 1–2 pounds of hair and soap sludge per person per year.

The good news: shower drain clogs are almost always within the first 2–3 feet of pipe, accessible without cutting walls or calling a plumber. This guide covers four methods, from 30-second fixes to thorough mechanical cleaning.

## Safety First

Do not pour chemical drain cleaner into a shower drain that has standing water with zero flow. The chemicals sit in the shower pan and do not reach the clog. If you have already used chemicals, wear rubber gloves and safety glasses before attempting any further work. Chemical drain cleaners (sodium hydroxide or sulfuric acid based) can cause severe skin and eye burns.

Baking soda and vinegar are safe, non-toxic, and will not damage pipes. They are always the right first chemical approach.

## Method 1: Remove Visible Hair With a Zip Tool
![Plastic hair removal tool for shower drains](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.-4AbnqvBZoI_caOBMIJBDAHaHX%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=be73e3cba8e2bc0faa669dd8a0c00e8edcf05bac4e871cd786706012d54f8604&ipo=images)

A plastic drain zip tool (also called a hair snake or drain stick) costs $3–5 for a pack of 2–3 and clears roughly 80% of shower clogs in under a minute. It is a 18–24 inch flexible plastic strip with barbs or teeth along the edges.

1. Remove the drain cover. Most shower drain covers pop off with a flathead screwdriver. Some have visible screws; others are held by friction-fit prongs. If your drain has a push-pull stopper, unscrew the stopper cap (turn counterclockwise) and remove it.
2. Feed the zip tool into the drain opening until you feel resistance—typically 6–18 inches down. The barbs face toward the pipe walls.
3. Twist the tool 2–3 full rotations to entangle hair on the barbs, then pull it straight out slowly. A clump of wet hair wrapped in grayish soap sludge will come out. This will smell. It is normal.
4. Repeat 3–4 times until the tool comes back clean. Run hot water for 30 seconds—if it drains freely, you are done.
5. Replace the drain cover.

If the zip tool comes back clean on the first pass and the drain is still slow, the clog is deeper or it is soap scum buildup rather than a hair mass.

## Method 2: Baking Soda and Vinegar
![Baking soda and vinegar drain cleaning process](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.EO2fm8VCgMxrVf9Iq3z4lgHaHg%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=62bd704ae73c36f54a6e24d68f0ae21e2b6dc8e853b36f1a661b699b6be82d3c&ipo=images)

This method works for soap scum buildup and deodorizing. The carbon dioxide bubbles mechanically scrub the pipe walls while the mild acid dissolves mineral deposits from hard water.

1. Remove standing water as much as possible—bail it into a bucket if the drain is fully blocked.
2. Pour ½ cup of baking soda directly into the drain opening. Tap the drain to help it settle.
3. Follow immediately with ½ cup of white vinegar. You will hear vigorous fizzing.
4. Cover the drain opening with a wet cloth or the stopper. The CO2 needs to expand down the pipe, not out the top.
5. Wait 15–20 minutes.
6. Flush with a full pot of boiling water (carefully pour—do not splash boiling water on yourself or an acrylic shower pan, which can warp from direct heat).

This method clears soap scum effectively but will not dissolve a dense hair clog. If the drain is still slow after this, the problem is mechanical—move to the plunger or snake.

## Method 3: The Plunger

A standard cup plunger (flat bottom) works for shower drains, not a toilet plunger (flanged). The key technique with shower drains is sealing the overflow opening.

1. If your shower has a tub spout or an overflow plate (a metal plate low on the wall, about 12 inches above the drain in tub-shower combos), seal it with a wet rag pressed firmly against the opening. Without this seal, the plunger pushes air out the overflow instead of water through the clog.
2. Add enough water to cover the plunger cup by 1–2 inches.
3. Place the plunger at an angle so the cup fills with water, then press it flat to form a seal over the drain.
4. Pump 10–15 times with firm, short strokes. On the last stroke, pull sharply to break the suction. The clog may release with a sudden gurgle.
5. If it drains, flush with hot water for 2–3 minutes.

## Method 4: Snake the Drain

If the zip tool and plunger both fail, the clog is beyond the reach of surface tools. A hand-crank drain snake ($15–25 for a 25-foot model) will reach it.

1. Remove the drain cover and any stopper mechanism. Some tub stoppers require removing the overflow plate and lifting out the linkage assembly—a brass rod with a spring and weight on the end—before you can pull the stopper from the drain. If you cannot figure out the stopper removal, search your specific brand or call a plumber rather than forcing it.
2. Feed the snake cable into the drain opening while turning the handle clockwise. You will feel the cable navigate the P-trap (the U-bend directly under the drain).
3. Continue feeding until you feel solid resistance. Push and turn simultaneously—the corkscrew tip grabs hair and soap scum.
4. Once you break through, continue feeding another 2–3 feet, then retract while turning. The cable will come back with the clog material wound around the tip.
5. Clean the cable with a rag as you retract it. Wear gloves—what comes out is unpleasant.
6. Reassemble the stopper mechanism and drain cover, then flush with hot water for 3–5 minutes.

If the snake goes beyond 15 feet and the drain is still slow, the problem may be in the main drain line, not the shower branch. This is the point to call a plumber ($150–$350).

## Prevention

- **Install a hair catcher.** A $5–10 silicone or stainless steel hair catcher sits over or inside the drain and catches hair before it enters the pipe. Clean it after every shower—it takes 5 seconds and prevents every clog. TubShroom and OXO Good Grips are the two most highly rated designs.
- **Brush hair before showering.** Loose hair that comes out during washing is the primary source of drain hair. Brushing before you get in the shower significantly reduces the amount that goes down the drain.
- **Monthly baking soda flush.** Once a month, pour ¼ cup baking soda followed by ¼ cup vinegar down the drain, wait 15 minutes, and flush with boiling water. This dissolves soap scum before it accumulates into a clog.
- **No bar soap in the shower if you have hard water.** Liquid body wash produces less soap scum than bar soap, especially in hard-water areas. If you cannot give up bar soap, install a water softener.
