## The Wall Switch Does NOT Isolate Power

![Three-way switch wiring showing common and traveler terminals](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.A5-eAh0gTPf30ffpT9ZfFwAAAA%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=006a30dc415c4962b238e271b8cdc1b242896db88979e7d736dade75533f1be5&ipo=images)

The single most dangerous assumption in switch replacement: "I turned the switch off, so the wires are dead." In the vast majority of homes, power is fed to the switch box first, then to the light. The switch interrupts only the hot wire—the incoming hot wire at the switch is always live when the breaker is on, regardless of switch position. Always turn off the breaker and verify with a voltage tester on the switch terminals. A non-contact tester costs $10 and takes 5 seconds to use. It is the difference between a safe repair and electrocution.

## Take a Photo Before Removing Any Wires

![Labeling switch wires before removal](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.gqKfcP8FklDgDpMAjnpBXQHaEg%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=8e94167d9c2c861efad81784bf16b2b98012ff2b5bbb2b03e0e559b34abf8714&ipo=images)

Before loosening a single screw, take a clear photo showing which wire goes to which terminal. For three-way switches, the dark-colored common screw is the critical one—mark that wire with masking tape before disconnecting. If you mix up the common and a traveler on a three-way, the switch will not work correctly from one or both locations, and the wiring diagram alone may not help because traveler wire colors are not standardized. A photo taken before disassembly solves the problem in 5 seconds.

## A Warm or Buzzing Switch Is a Fire Hazard

![Burnt or damaged light switch indicating replacement needed](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.crWE7BN6lQdaB0_3N3B06wHaE1%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=18148ed8519cd778095538a7814df13524a240d4565064dfbb923c506998cb00&ipo=images)

A switch that is warm to the touch, buzzes, crackles, or shows scorch marks is not just annoying—it is a fire hazard. Heat indicates high electrical resistance at a loose or corroded connection inside the switch. Replace it immediately. When you remove the old switch, inspect the wire ends: if the copper is discolored (dark brown, blue, or black rather than bright copper), the wire has been overheating. Cut back the damaged section and strip fresh copper. If the wire is too short to cut back, the heat damage may extend into the wall—an electrician should evaluate.

## Use the Screw Terminals, Not Backstabs

Replace the new switch using the screw terminals, even if the old switch used backstab connections. Backstabs rely on a tiny spring clip to hold the wire—they are faster to install but less reliable over time. Form a clockwise hook in the stripped copper with pliers, loop it around the terminal screw, and tighten firmly. The wire should wrap at least two-thirds around the screw. Tug each wire to confirm it is secure. This takes 30 extra seconds per wire and eliminates the most common cause of intermittent switch failure.

## Check for a Neutral Before Buying a Smart Switch

Many smart switches, dimmers, and motion-sensor switches require a neutral wire (white) in the switch box to power their internal electronics. Open the box and look for a bundle of white wires capped together with a wire nut. If present, you have a neutral and can install most smart switches. If the box contains only black wires (and possibly a red for three-way), you have no neutral—common in homes wired before NEC began requiring neutrals in switch boxes in 2011. Without a neutral, you need a "no-neutral-required" smart switch (Lutron Caseta is the most reliable brand for this scenario) or you must run a new cable, which requires opening walls.
