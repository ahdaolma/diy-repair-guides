## Identify Your Operator Before You Buy Anything

![Window crank operator types comparison](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.tdhAbDXk_7RXWIQcMu1gIwHaEK%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=85ab36698b827b86df826066123c2ebf7d4204adb5451306aa7ea7560292d200&ipo=images)

Casement window operators come in four main types, and they are NOT interchangeable. Buying the wrong one means an extra trip to the hardware store and a window that stays stuck.

**Single-arm operator (most common, 1980-present):** One metal arm extends from the gear housing to a track on the sash. Found on Truth, Andersen, Pella, and most post-1980 casement windows. The arm has a small plastic clip or metal pin at the tip that slides in the sash track. When the gears strip, the crank spins but the arm does not move.

**Dual-arm operator (older, pre-1980):** Two arms extend from the housing—one connects to the sash, one to the frame. Found on older Andersen and Pella windows. More complicated to replace because both arms must align simultaneously.

**Split-arm operator (awning windows):** The arm splits into two pieces that push the sash outward from the bottom. Used on awning and hopper windows. Not the same as casement operators.

**T-bar / cross-pin operator (vintage, pre-1970):** Uses a T-shaped pin or cross-pin instead of a splined shaft. The crank handle has a corresponding slot. These are increasingly hard to find—Prime-Line H 3525 is the universal replacement.

### How to identify yours without removing it

Take a photo of the operator from underneath (inside the window frame, looking up). Note the brand stamped on the metal arm or housing. Measure the arm length from the center of the gear to the tip when fully extended. Count the number of mounting screw holes. With these three data points—brand, arm length, screw pattern—any hardware store associate can find your replacement in under 30 seconds.

## Step-by-Step Replacement (15 Minutes)

![Window crank removal process](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.hVNju_-4w3ghfP9lyK3iMgHaE8%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=c167c0b29cc13eeee29ff96327c991a147a0506895ab90f91c89b7fc93272156&ipo=images)

1. **Remove the crank handle.** Find the set screw on the underside of the handle where it meets the operator shaft. Use a flathead screwdriver or Allen wrench (typically 1/8-inch or 3/32-inch). The screw is small, magnetic, and easy to drop—hold a magnet near it as you loosen.

2. **Unscrew the operator from the window frame.** Two to four Phillips-head screws hold the operator to the sill or frame. Remove them. If rusted, apply penetrating oil (PB Blaster, not WD-40) and wait 5 minutes before trying again.

3. **Disconnect the arm from the sash track.** Slide the sash open slightly. Look for a plastic clip, metal pin, or snap-in connector at the tip of the arm where it meets the track. Most modern operators (post-1990) use a snap-clip that releases by pressing a tab. Older ones use a screw or rivet.

4. **Remove the old operator.** Once the screws and arm are disconnected, lift the operator out of the frame. Note the orientation—the new one must face the same direction.

5. **Install the new operator in reverse order.** Mount the housing. Connect the arm tip to the sash track. Test-crank before tightening the mounting screws fully to confirm the arm travels smoothly through its full range. Then tighten everything and reinstall the handle.

## Quick Diagnosis Chart

| Symptom | Most Likely Cause | Fix | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Crank spins freely, window does not move | Stripped operator gears | Replace operator | $20-50 |
| Crank is extremely stiff, grinds | Dried grease in operator or dirty hinge track | Clean and lubricate first. Replace operator if no improvement. | $5-50 |
| Window will not close fully | Arm bent, sash misaligned, or track obstruction | Inspect arm for bends. Adjust sash hinges. | $0-20 |
| Handle falls off repeatedly | Stripped set screw threads | Replace handle ($8-15) or use thread locker | $8-15 |
| Rusted, screeching noise | Hinges or operator corroded | Replace hinge set if rusted through; lubricate operator | $5-50 |
| Crank works but window moves unevenly | One hinge broken or bent | Replace hinge pair ($25-50 per pair) | $25-50 |

## The Lubrication Trick That Saves $50

Many operators labeled "broken" are just dry. Before replacing a stiff crank:

1. Remove the handle.
2. Spray silicone lubricant (not WD-40—it evaporates and leaves no lasting film) directly into the gear housing around the shaft.
3. Crank the shaft back and forth 10-15 times with pliers to work the lubricant into the gear teeth.
4. If the crank smooths out, you just saved $20-50. Reinstall the handle and enjoy.
5. If it is still stiff after lubrication, the gears are physically worn (missing teeth). Replace the operator.

**Lubrication rules for all window hardware:**
- Silicone spray for operator gears and arm pivot points
- White lithium grease for hinge tracks
- Dry graphite for lock mechanisms (never oil locks—oil attracts dust and gums up)
- Never lubricate the set screw—it needs friction to stay tight
