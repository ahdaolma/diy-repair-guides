## Diagnose the Cause First
![Removing a damaged floor tile with chisel](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.RIaJt_fYEDPjhsnoB6kaqQHaE7%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=c715226371b705193f382cda5ff4bb62e8940deebab14dd6eeecf4c39236af03&ipo=images)

A cracked or loose floor tile is a symptom, not necessarily the root problem. Tile cracks for three reasons: impact (dropped object), substrate movement (the subfloor flexes, the rigid tile cannot), or improper installation (voids in the thinset mortar beneath the tile). Before replacing the tile, determine which:

- **Impact crack (single tile, starburst pattern from impact point):** Simple replacement. The subfloor is fine.
- **Crack across multiple tiles in a line:** The subfloor or underlayment has a joint or crack below that is telegraphing through. Replacing the tiles without addressing the substrate means the new tiles crack in the same place.
- **Hollow sound when tapped (single tile):** The thinset bond has failed. The tile is loose but intact. It needs to be removed, the old thinset cleaned off, and the tile re-set.
- **Hollow sound across multiple tiles, with cracking:** Subfloor deflection. The floor is not stiff enough for tile. This requires subfloor reinforcement—a major project.

## Removing a Damaged Tile
![Setting a new floor tile in thinset mortar](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.bj-opkVhy0zKUa8NDJHZXQHaEK%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=04a761d611b3e1737e2470a316d565de818d4b343f10217c285418039431f0b0&ipo=images)

1. Protect adjacent tiles: lay painter's tape over the edges of the surrounding tiles where you will be working. A slipped chisel or hammer can chip a good tile.
2. Remove the grout around the damaged tile using a grout saw or an oscillating multi-tool with a carbide grout blade. You must sever the grout bond on all four sides—otherwise, prying up the tile can chip adjacent tiles.
3. Drill relief holes: drill several holes through the damaged tile with a ¼-inch masonry bit. This weakens the tile and makes it break into controllable pieces rather than shattering unpredictably.
4. Place a cold chisel in the center of the tile at a low angle. Tap with a hammer. The tile should crack and break into pieces.
5. Remove the broken pieces. Start from the center and work outward toward the edges, being careful not to pry against adjacent tiles.
6. Chisel out the old thinset mortar down to the substrate. Use a hammer and chisel, or a rotary hammer drill with a chisel bit for larger jobs. The surface must be flat and clean—new thinset will not bond to old thinset ridges.
7. Vacuum all dust and debris.
8. If the substrate is a cement backer board and it is damaged during removal, patch with thinset before proceeding.

## Installing the Replacement Tile
![Grouting around a newly installed floor tile](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.explicit.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP._t26vA8ukv95EXRc8vlYZQHaLG%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=53eb60824188fe479fecd7d42d374284c2fc38859f07b9aec84c0f950c606194&ipo=images)

1. Dry-fit the replacement tile. It should sit flush with surrounding tiles. If it sits proud (above), chisel more thinset from the substrate. If it sits below, you will need to build up with extra thinset.
2. Mix thinset mortar per package directions. For a single tile, a small 10-pound box ($8–12) is sufficient. The consistency should be like creamy peanut butter.
3. Apply thinset to the substrate with a notched trowel (¼ × ¼ inch notch). Comb the thinset in one direction—the ridges collapse uniformly when the tile is pressed in.
4. Back-butter the tile: apply a thin, flat layer of thinset to the back of the tile with the flat side of the trowel. This fills any texture on the tile back and ensures 100% coverage—the industry standard for floors.
5. Press the tile into place. Wiggle it slightly perpendicular to the trowel ridges to collapse them. Use tile spacers to maintain consistent grout joints with surrounding tiles.
6. Check for level: lay a straightedge or level across the new tile and adjacent tiles. It should be dead flush. If not, lift the tile, adjust thinset thickness, and reset.
7. Let the thinset cure for 24 hours before walking on the tile.

## Grouting the Replacement Tile

1. Remove spacers.
2. Mix grout (match the existing grout color and type—sanded for joints over ⅛ inch, unsanded for narrower). Take a chip of old grout to the store for color matching.
3. Force grout into the joints with a grout float, working diagonally across the tile.
4. Sponge off excess with a damp (not wet) sponge after 15–30 minutes.
5. Buff haze with a dry cloth after 2–3 hours.
6. Let grout cure for 72 hours, then seal.

## If You Do Not Have a Matching Replacement Tile

- Check the attic, basement, or garage for leftover tiles from the original installation. Builders and previous owners often store extras.
- Check the back of a loose tile (if you have one) or inside a cabinet toe-kick for the manufacturer and color code. Tile lines are discontinued frequently—the manufacturer may have a close match in a current line.
- If no match exists: remove a tile from an inconspicuous location (inside a closet, under an appliance) and use it to replace the visible damaged tile. Install the closest-match replacement in the hidden spot.

## Epoxy Repair for Small Chips

For small chips (under ¼ inch) in a tile that is otherwise intact:
1. Clean the chipped area with alcohol.
2. Mix two-part epoxy tinted to match the tile color (use a small amount of acrylic paint or purchase a tile repair kit, $10–15).
3. Apply the tinted epoxy to the chip with a toothpick. Overfill slightly.
4. After curing (5–30 minutes depending on the epoxy), sand flush with 400-grit wet/dry sandpaper.
5. Apply a thin coat of clear nail polish or clear epoxy over the repair for gloss matching.
