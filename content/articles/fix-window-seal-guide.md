## Why Window Seals Fail
![Applying weatherstripping to window frame](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.v51n76iJY3P-ZTNjPCbNegHaEg%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=8efd31e80127a410bbfa940f40fa074acf20938fd442529b9158e6a1a035e1bd&ipo=images)

The Department of Energy estimates that windows account for 10–25% of a home's heating and cooling energy loss. Over time, the materials that seal operable windows—felt, foam, rubber gaskets, and caulk—compress, harden, crack, and lose their seal. The result is drafts you can feel, higher energy bills, and condensation between double-pane glass (a sign the factory seal between panes has failed, which is not fixable—the entire glass unit or window must be replaced).

This guide covers sealing the gap between the sash and frame (the draft you feel) and the gap between the frame and the rough opening (the hidden leak behind trim). The materials cost $10–$30 per window.

## Find the Leaks First

On a cold, windy day, close all windows and turn on exhaust fans to depressurize the house. Hold a lit incense stick or smoke pen near all edges of the window—where the sashes meet, where the sash meets the frame, and around the trim. Smoke movement reveals exactly where air is infiltrating. Mark leak locations with painter's tape.

## Fix 1: V-Strip Weatherstripping for Sash Channels
![Caulking gap around window frame](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.OuQBgXBryMXfqfY4sK9NUwHaEl%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=51e9a9ee4f23a87d61dd7bc6cdd771a88510d8205f96ffd761dedffb0e75f9db&ipo=images)

For double-hung and sliding windows, V-strip (also called tension seal or V-channel) is a vinyl or bronze strip folded into a V-shape that compresses when the window closes.

1. Clean the sash channel thoroughly with alcohol. Adhesive will not bond to dust or grease.
2. Cut V-strip to the height of the sash channel.
3. Peel the adhesive backing and press the V-strip into the channel with the open side of the V facing outward (toward the sash, not the frame). The sash slides against the V-strip, compressing it to create a seal.
4. Apply to both sides and the top of the sash. The bottom seal is typically a different type (see below).
5. For bronze V-strip (more durable, 20+ year lifespan): it is nailed in place with small tacks every 4–6 inches rather than using adhesive. Bronze is the professional choice for historic wood windows.

## Fix 2: Adhesive Foam Tape for Meeting Rails and Stops

The meeting rail is where the upper and lower sashes meet in the middle. The window stops are the wood strips the sash presses against when closed.

1. Clean surfaces with alcohol.
2. Apply closed-cell foam tape (not open-cell—open cell absorbs water and freezes) to the meeting rail, window stops, and the bottom of the lower sash.
3. Foam tape compresses permanently and typically lasts 2–3 years before needing replacement. It is the cheapest option at $3–5 per roll but the shortest-lived.

## Fix 3: Rope Caulk for Seasonal Sealing

Rope caulk (also called removable weatherstrip caulk) is a clay-like compound that comes in a roll. You press it into gaps by hand and it peels off cleanly in spring.

1. Tear off a piece and roll it into a rope of the needed thickness.
2. Press it firmly into the gap between the sash and frame, or between the upper and lower sashes.
3. This is ideal for windows you do not open during winter. It seals irregular gaps that foam tape cannot, and it removes completely. Cost: $5–8 per roll. One roll seals 3–4 average windows.

## Fix 4: Caulk the Frame-to-Wall Gap
![Using incense to detect window draft](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.dSh0IFUflh5wI6TB4Ga0cgHaEl%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=59005e5e6dc5a08abfd5872b4bb1b2e72ab66197fc5189e7b76242cea251f691&ipo=images)

The gap between the window frame and the rough opening is hidden behind trim. It is often uninsulated and unsealed—a direct air path from outside to inside.

1. Carefully remove the interior window trim using a flat pry bar. Work slowly and protect the wall with a putty knife behind the bar.
2. Inspect the gap. It may be stuffed with fiberglass insulation (which filters air but does not stop it) or completely empty.
3. Fill the gap with low-expansion window and door spray foam (Great Stuff Window & Door). Standard foam exerts too much pressure and can bow the window frame.
4. Let the foam cure, trim excess with a utility knife, and reinstall the trim. Caulk the trim-to-wall joint with paintable acrylic caulk.

## Fix 5: Window Insulation Film

For single-pane or severely drafty windows that you will not replace immediately, window insulation film ($10–20 per kit, covers 3–5 windows) creates an additional air barrier.

1. Apply double-sided tape around the window frame.
2. Press the plastic film onto the tape.
3. Heat the film with a hair dryer—it shrinks drum-tight and becomes nearly invisible.
4. The trapped air space between the film and the glass provides insulation roughly equivalent to a second pane of glass. The DOE estimates window film can reduce window heat loss by 30–50%.

This is a seasonal solution—install in fall, remove in spring. It renders the window inoperable for the winter.

## When the Seal Is Failed Between Glass Panes

If you see fog, condensation, or haze between the panes of a double-pane (insulated glass) window that cannot be wiped off, the factory hermetic seal has failed. Moisture has entered the air space between the panes, and the insulating argon or krypton gas has escaped. This cannot be repaired—the glass unit must be replaced. Options:

- Replace the insulated glass unit (IGU) only: $150–$300 per window, keeps the existing frame. This is the most common repair.
- Replace the entire window: $400–$1,000 per window installed. Worth it if the frame is also damaged or the window is 30+ years old.
- Defogging services exist but are widely considered temporary—they drill small holes, clean the interior, and install vents. The insulating gas is permanently lost, and the seal is not restored.
