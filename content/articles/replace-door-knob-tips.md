## The Only Measurement That Matters: Backset

![Door knob backset measurement](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.4j2p0lSJu0EWgfoT_1x9sAHaC0%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=38ffd45fdf5540333f5274c4afa54053eeeb93a504565269bf0a55bd04fa34b0&ipo=images)

Backset is the distance from the edge of the door to the center of the knob hole. It is either 2-3/8 inches (standard) or 2-3/4 inches (less common, older homes). Buying a 2-3/8-inch knob for a 2-3/4-inch door means the latch will not reach the strike plate and the door will not close.

Measure from the door edge to the center of the existing knob hole. If replacing a knob, measure the old one—do not assume. Most modern knobs (Schlage, Kwikset, Defiant, Weiser) come with an adjustable latch that flips between 2-3/8 and 2-3/4 inches. Look for the adjustable latch plate when buying.

## Knob Types and Brands

| Type | Price Range | Best For | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Passage (hall/closet) | $10-25 | Closets, hallways, rooms without locks | No locking mechanism |
| Privacy (bedroom/bathroom) | $15-30 | Bedrooms, bathrooms | Push-button or twist lock on inside, emergency release pinhole on outside |
| Keyed entry | $25-50 | Exterior doors, home offices | Key on outside, thumb turn on inside |
| Dummy knob | $8-15 | Closet double doors, decorative | No latch—surface mount only, purely decorative |
| Smart lock | $100-300 | Front door | Keypad, Bluetooth, Wi-Fi. Requires 2-1/8-inch cross bore |

**Brand quality tiers:**
- **Budget (Defiant, Gatehouse, Generic):** $10-15. Works fine for closets and low-traffic doors. The finish wears off within 2-3 years of daily use. The internal spring mechanism feels loose from day one.
- **Mid-range (Kwikset, Weiser):** $20-35. Good for bedroom and bathroom doors. Kwikset's SmartKey feature lets you re-key the lock yourself in 30 seconds without a locksmith—useful if you want all interior knobs keyed alike.
- **Premium (Schlage, Baldwin, Emtek):** $40-80+. Heavier, tighter tolerances, finishes that last 15+ years. The internal mechanism feels solid—no wobble, no rattle. Worth the money for any door you touch daily.

## 15-Minute Installation

![Door knob installation process](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.explicit.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP._XflXJPI3Abv4QsxLQrnUwHaEK%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=0f6240cc6f110dd5df2f5ac515242666f01333822ae8d9a8db4d554aeaf8618a&ipo=images)

1. **Remove the old knob.** Look for a small slot or hole on the interior knob collar (where the knob meets the rose plate). Insert a flathead screwdriver or the included removal tool and push the retaining pin. The knob pulls off. Unscrew the two screws holding the rose plate. Pull the latch mechanism out of the door edge. Remove the strike plate from the door frame (two screws).

2. **Check the door prep.** Standard doors have a 2-1/8-inch cross bore (the big hole through the door face) and a 1-inch edge bore (the hole in the door edge for the latch). If your door does not have these holes, you need a door installation kit with hole saws—this is a whole different project.

3. **Adjust the latch backset.** On the new latch assembly, look for the adjustable plate. It slides between 2-3/8 and 2-3/4 inches. Set it to match your measurement. The latch bevel (the angled side of the latch bolt) must face the direction the door closes. If the bevel faces the wrong way, the latch will bang against the strike plate instead of sliding into it.

4. **Install the latch.** Slide the latch into the edge bore. Screw it in with the two provided screws. Do not overtighten—the latch plate should sit flush but not bend.

5. **Install the knobs.** Slide the exterior knob through the latch mechanism. Slide the interior knob onto the other side. The two halves snap together. Tighten the two screws on the interior rose plate.

6. **Test before installing the strike plate.** Close the door. Mark where the latch bolt touches the door frame—this is where the strike plate goes. If the latch hits at the right height but the door will not stay closed, the strike plate is misaligned vertically. See the adjustment section below.

## Strike Plate Alignment: The #1 Problem

![Door latch and strike plate alignment](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.AcqO0IegrfYD7W-mhAnxCAHaDh%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=19825ffbd800ca597b4af9c0e517e5da97d387633d77585b7cd6c7f8ea1570b6&ipo=images)

The strike plate is the metal plate on the door frame that the latch slides into. If it is even 1/16-inch misaligned, the door will not latch properly. Three fixes, in order of least to most invasive:

**Fix 1: Enlarge the strike hole with a file.** If the latch hits too high or low by less than 1/8-inch, remove the strike plate and file the opening larger in the direction needed. A round metal file costs $5 and 5 minutes of filing usually solves it.

**Fix 2: Bend the strike plate tab.** Most strike plates have a small metal tab inside the opening. Bend this tab slightly inward (toward the door stop) with pliers. This reduces the gap between the latch and the strike, making the door close tighter.

**Fix 3: Move the strike plate.** Remove the strike plate. Fill the old screw holes with toothpicks and wood glue. Drill new pilot holes for the correct position. If the misalignment is vertical and the door sags, the problem is the hinges, not the strike plate—see door frame repair.

## Pro Tips

![Finished door knob installation](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.Ld_oOO9ku_htWLxTYHxdMAHaEK%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=44f7d192592a36b2e8fa18c98871685761093d4d5a0d0d7235cccfd19f111871&ipo=images)

**Tighten everything after one week.** New knobs settle. The screws that mount the rose plate will loosen slightly. Tighten them after one week of use and they will stay tight for years.

**Graphite, not oil.** If the latch mechanism feels stiff, puff a small amount of powdered graphite (about $3 at any hardware store) into the latch opening. Graphite lubricates without attracting dust. Never use WD-40 or oil on a door latch—it gums up with dust within months.

**Match all knobs in the same room.** If your bedroom door and closet door are next to each other, use the same brand and finish. Different brands have slightly different finishes even when both are labeled "satin nickel" or "oil-rubbed bronze." The difference is invisible across the house but obvious on adjacent doors.

**Save the old strike plate.** The new knob comes with a strike plate, but the old one is already painted to match the door frame and the screw holes line up. If the new latch works with the old strike plate, keep the old one installed—less painting and patching.
