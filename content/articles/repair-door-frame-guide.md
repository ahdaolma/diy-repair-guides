## Your Door Isn't Broken — The Frame Is

![Stripped hinge screw repair kit](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.ZDePSoDe6klryHqlerLm1wHaHo%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=c0b85a5eb9b01cfff2dd4f49a7abaa6276f85623f6675239845961209b139156&ipo=images)

When an interior door sags and scrapes the floor, or won't latch because the strike plate has shifted, the problem is almost never the door. Wood doors don't grow or shrink along their length. The problem is the door frame — specifically, the hinge screw holes in the jamb have stripped out, the wood has split, or the jamb itself has shifted. Replacing an interior pre-hung door costs $150-$400. Fixing the frame costs $5-$30.

## Fix 1: Stripped Hinge Screw Holes (90% of Sagging Doors)

![How to fix stripped screw holes in wood door frame](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.BC3Asi07hlo7EDxBls5iagHaE8%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=8a281f5726a3cb9e26993efd1b29e2d98107cf2cac8a342b0163bdbb479c2f5&ipo=images)

Over years of opening and closing, the 3/4-inch screws that come with hinges slowly wallow out the wood — the screw holes become oval, the screws lose grip, the hinge pulls away from the jamb by 1/16 to 1/8 inch, and the door sags enough to scrape the floor.

**Three fixes ranked by permanence:**

**Option A — Longer screws (temporary fix, 5 minutes, $0):** Replace one short hinge screw on each hinge with a 2.5-inch or 3-inch wood screw ($5/box of 100). The longer screw drives through the jamb and into the framing (the king stud or jack stud) behind it. This pulls the jamb tight against the framing and often solves the sag immediately. Use ONLY on the jamb side of the hinge, not the door side — the door is only 1-3/8 inches thick. One long screw per hinge, driven through the center screw hole. This is what every handyman does as a first step.

**Option B — Wooden dowel repair (permanent, 30 minutes, $3):**
1. Remove the hinge screws and the hinge from the jamb.
2. Drill out each stripped screw hole with a 3/8-inch drill bit to a depth of 1.5 inches. This creates a clean, round hole.
3. Cut a 3/8-inch hardwood dowel ($3 for a 3-foot length at Home Depot) into 1.5-inch lengths. Coat each dowel piece with wood glue (Titebond II, $5).
4. Tap the glued dowels into the drilled holes with a hammer. Let the glue dry for 2 hours (or 30 minutes if you're impatient — the screws are the mechanical fastener, the glue is backup).
5. Cut the dowels flush with the jamb using a flush-cut pull saw ($12 — cuts flush without marring the surrounding wood). Or use a utility knife with multiple passes.
6. Drill new pilot holes (1/8-inch bit for #8 screws, the standard hinge screw size) in the center of each dowel.
7. Reinstall the hinges. The screws bite into fresh, solid hardwood instead of wallowed-out soft pine jamb wood. This is a permanent repair — it's stronger than the original.

**Option C — Hinge repair plates (permanent, 5 minutes, $8):**
Prime-Line hinge repair brackets ($8 for a pack of 2 at Home Depot) — thin metal plates with pre-drilled screw holes that screw over the damaged hinge mortise. They distribute the hinge load across a larger area of the jamb. Paint them to match the door frame. This is the fastest permanent fix for severely damaged jambs where the wood around the hinge has split.

## Fix 2: Split Jamb Wood

![Door frame jamb repair with wood glue](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.LU88u2OAQK0zNpxHpsgb5gHaEK%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=b19edf9a165e7e6fe0571ecc18c3ee3ecc28be5e42a980f80166041357cf5367&ipo=images)

If the jamb wood has physically split (a crack running from the hinge screw into the jamb), the wood fibers themselves have separated. Screws in split wood have zero holding power.

1. Inject wood glue into the crack with the nozzle of the glue bottle forced against the crack. Flex the wood open and closed to pump the glue deeper into the crack.
2. Clamp the crack shut with a bar clamp or a C-clamp, using scrap wood pads between the clamp jaws and the finished jamb surface to prevent marring. Wait 2 hours for Titebond II to cure (24 hours for full strength).
3. After the glue cures, proceed with the dowel repair (Option B above) for the stripped screw holes — the split wood repair restores the wood structure, and the dowel repair provides new solid wood for the screws to bite into.

## Fix 3: Rotted Door Jamb Bottom (Exterior Doors)

Exterior door jambs rot at the bottom 6-12 inches where rain splashes up from the threshold. The wood becomes soft and crumbly — you can push a screwdriver into it with finger pressure. If the rot is less than 6 inches and only affecting the bottom of the jamb:

1. Cut out the rotted section with an oscillating multi-tool ($30 for a basic model at Harbor Freight). Cut straight across at the top of the rot. Remove the entire rotted piece — you need to reach solid wood.
2. Cut a replacement piece from pressure-treated 2x stock or PVC trim board (Azek, $15 for a 1x4x8). PVC will never rot — it's the permanent fix.
3. Glue and screw the replacement piece to the remaining sound wood. Prime and paint all exposed wood surfaces (including the cut end of the original jamb) with exterior primer before assembly — end grain absorbs water like a straw.
4. Caulk all joints with exterior silicone caulk.

If the rot extends more than 12 inches up the jamb, or into the threshold/subfloor beneath the door, the door needs full replacement — the rot has compromised the structural framing around the door.

## Fix 4: Strike Plate Misaligned — Door Won't Latch

When the door latch bolt no longer aligns with the strike plate hole, the door bounces open with a push. This usually follows hinge sag — the door has dropped, so the latch is now 1/8 inch below the strike plate hole.

**Fix 1 — Tighten hinges (5 minutes):** The #1 cause. Tighten all hinge screws. If they spin without tightening, use the long screw method (Fix 1, Option A). A sagging door that returns to its original position after tightening the hinges often aligns the strike plate automatically.

**Fix 2 — File the strike plate (10 minutes):** If the latch is catching on the bottom edge of the strike plate hole, file the hole 1/16 inch larger at the bottom with a metal file ($5). Remove the strike plate from the jamb, clamp it in a vise, file the bottom edge, reinstall.

**Fix 3 — Move the strike plate (30 minutes):** If the latch misalignment is more than 1/8 inch:
1. Remove the strike plate. Fill the old screw holes with glued dowels (same technique as hinge repair).
2. Hold the strike plate in the new position. Trace its outline with a pencil.
3. Chisel out the new mortise (the recessed area the strike plate sits in) to the depth of the strike plate thickness — typically 1/16 inch. Use a sharp 1/2-inch wood chisel ($10).
4. Drill new pilot holes. Reinstall the strike plate. Fill the gap from the old mortise with wood filler ($5), sand, and paint.
