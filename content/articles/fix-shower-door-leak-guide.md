## Water on Your Bathroom Floor = Rot, Mold, and $$$

![Fix leaking shower door seal](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthfvnext.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.rSbDnRTDv7gTj-LseljCwAHaEK%3Fr%3D0%26cb%3Dthfvnextfalcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=4c6939f78352ed06255a0799d8efc9b8875fb9e87482d8bb8d7d36853dcb9a06&ipo=images)

A shower door that leaks a cup of water onto the bathroom floor per shower doesn't seem urgent — until the water seeps under the baseboard, rots the drywall, delaminates the flooring, and feeds a black mold colony in the wall cavity. A 2023 insurance industry report found that shower enclosure leaks are the #3 source of bathroom water damage claims, averaging $2,800 per incident. Most of those claims started with a shower door that "just leaked a little."

Shower doors leak from exactly three places. All three are fixable in under an hour with basic tools:

---

## Leak Point 1: The Bottom Sweep/Drip Rail (80% of Leaks)

![Replace shower door bottom sweep](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthfvnext.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.NSLNsXNQuBDm6k2vxY4r1wHaD4%3Fr%3D0%26cb%3Dthfvnextfalcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=b7b0af093c4fb619f57e06021f04f50dbc07b87901fed4ee978dccf97711df59&ipo=images)

The clear or white vinyl strip on the bottom of the shower door — the sweep — directs water that hits the door back into the shower. Over 5-8 years, the vinyl hardens, cracks, and curls. Once the sweep is curled upward, it catches water and directs it OUT of the shower instead of in.

**Fix (15 minutes, $8-$15):**

1. Identify the sweep type. There are three:
   - **U-channel snap-on:** A flexible vinyl piece that snaps onto the bottom rail of the door. Most common on framed shower doors. Replacement: Prime-Line M 6224 or M 6226 ($8 at Home Depot). These are universal and cut to length with scissors.
   - **T-shaped push-in:** A rigid vinyl strip with a T-shaped top that slides into a slot in the bottom of frameless glass doors. Replacement: CRL T-Sweep ($12). Remove the old one by pulling it out of the slot — it may be stiff. Press the new one in firmly along the entire length.
   - **Drip rail with sweep:** A metal channel screwed to the bottom of the door with a vinyl sweep inserted into it. If the sweep is cracked but the metal rail is intact, replace just the vinyl insert ($6). If the metal rail is rusted, replace the entire assembly ($20-$30, Delta or Kohler brand-specific).

2. Clean the bottom rail thoroughly — soap scum, hard water deposits, and old adhesive must be removed for the new sweep to seat properly. Use a plastic scraper (not metal — it scratches the metal rail) and CLR cleaner ($5).

3. For a square-cut snap-on sweep: cut to the exact width of the door with heavy-duty scissors. Start from one end and press it onto the rail, working your way across. For frameless door sweeps: cut 1/4 inch shorter than the door width to allow for expansion.

4. Test: run the shower and watch the bottom of the door. Water should hit the sweep and drip down into the shower. If water is still escaping UNDER the sweep, the sweep is too short (not contacting the threshold) — replace with a taller profile.

## Leak Point 2: The Side Seals/Vertical Jamb Seals (15% of Leaks)

![Shower door vertical seal replacement](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthfvnext.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.8GSXwYAQDPTULdSdEir1EgHaHp%3Fr%3D0%26cb%3Dthfvnextfalcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=494e04b48f6e1431a9aa97d05859a7823ffe622bd1324d29627ced4f4cebf569&ipo=images)

The vertical vinyl or rubber seals on the hinged side and the strike side (where the door meets the wall or the other door panel) compress when the door closes, creating a watertight barrier. When these seals harden, tear, or develop gaps, water sprays through the gap directly onto the floor.

**Fix (20 minutes, $10-$20):**

1. Inspect the seal by closing the door and looking for gaps — shine a flashlight from inside the shower and look for light coming through.
2. The vertical seal is typically a magnetic vinyl strip (on framed doors) or a clear vinyl U-channel (on frameless doors). Frameless door seals: CRL Frameless Shower Door Seal ($12 for a 36-inch length) or Prime-Line M 6223 ($10). Framed door magnetic seals: Prime-Line M 6228 ($15). These are universal parts that fit most standard doors.
3. Remove the old seal: it's either pressed into a channel in the door frame or glued to the glass edge. If glued, use a plastic razor blade ($5) to scrape off old adhesive without scratching the glass. Clean with rubbing alcohol.
4. Cut the new seal to length. Press into the metal channel (for framed doors) or adhere to the glass edge with the pre-applied adhesive strip (for frameless — clean the glass edge thoroughly first for adhesion).
5. For magnetic seals: the magnet in the seal pulls it against the metal door frame, creating a positive seal. Ensure the magnetic side faces the metal — install it backward and it repels instead of attracting.

## Leak Point 3: The Frame-to-Wall/Tile Caulk Joint (5% of Leaks)

Where the shower door frame meets the tile wall, a bead of silicone caulk seals the gap. Silicone lasts 5-10 years before it cracks, separates, and allows water to run down the outside of the frame onto the floor. This looks like water pooling at the base of the door frame on the OUTSIDE of the shower.

**Fix (1 hour including drying time, $10):**

1. Cut out ALL the old caulk with a utility knife and a caulk removal tool ($5). You cannot caulk over old caulk — silicone won't bond to cured silicone. Remove every trace.
2. Clean the joint with rubbing alcohol or mineral spirits. Let dry completely (30 minutes minimum — moisture prevents silicone from curing).
3. Apply 100% silicone caulk labeled for kitchen and bath (GE Silicone II, $7/tube). NOT acrylic caulk — acrylic absorbs water and molds in shower environments.
4. Cut the nozzle at a 45-degree angle, small opening (1/8 inch). Apply a continuous bead, pushing the caulk INTO the joint rather than laying it on top.
5. Immediately tool the bead with a wet finger or a caulk smoothing tool ($3) — press firmly to force caulk into the gap and create a concave surface that sheds water.
6. Do not use the shower for 24 hours. Silicone needs 24 hours to fully cure. Moisture during curing causes adhesion failure and the caulk peels off within weeks.

## Pro Tips

- If you replace the bottom sweep and water STILL leaks, the problem may be the shower threshold (the raised lip at the bottom of the door opening). If the threshold is cracked or the caulk around it has failed, water seeps under the threshold and onto the floor. Remove and replace the threshold caulk using the same 100% silicone method.
- Frameless glass doors that have shifted out of alignment over time — the hinges loosen, the door sags, and gaps appear at the strike side. Realign by loosening the hinge screws, repositioning the door so it's parallel with the wall, and retightening. This is a two-person job — the glass is heavy and tempered glass CANNOT be repaired if dropped.
- If water is leaking from the TOP of a frameless door — where the glass meets the wall at the hinge — the door was installed without a top drip rail. A universal drip rail ($25, CRL) directs water from the top of the door back into the shower. Most frameless door leaks at the top are solved with this single addition.
