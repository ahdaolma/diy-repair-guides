## Your Toilet Is the #1 Water Waster in Your Home

![Toilet flapper and fill valve replacement](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.QYwIZN7ML9s_KhXHPLrW5QHaHa%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=99af59017e1e6a326ef44719de2ab25b3a0e0dc2abd68a77744e130a98d452e9&ipo=images)

A running toilet — one where you hear water trickling or the tank periodically refills without anyone flushing — wastes 200-600 gallons per day according to the EPA WaterSense program. At the national average water rate of $0.005 per gallon, that's $1-$3 per day, $365-$1,095 per year — literally money going down the drain. The fix is almost always a $10 flapper and 10 minutes of work.

Toilets are mechanically simple. There are only four moving parts inside the tank: the flush handle/lever, the flapper (or flush valve seal), the fill valve, and the float. Every common toilet problem traces to one of these four parts. None require removing the toilet from the floor.

## Problem 1: Toilet Runs Continuously — Water Trickling Into the Bowl

**Diagnose:** Put food coloring (5 drops) in the tank. Wait 15 minutes WITHOUT flushing. If colored water appears in the bowl, the flapper is leaking.

**Cause:** The flapper (the rubber seal at the bottom of the tank that lifts when you flush) has worn out, warped, or accumulated mineral deposits that prevent a tight seal. Flappers last 3-5 years. Chlorine in municipal water and drop-in bleach tablets in the tank accelerate flapper degradation (bleach tablets in the tank are the #1 cause of premature flapper failure — never use them).

**Fix (10 minutes, $10):**
1. Turn off the water supply valve behind the toilet (oval handle, turn clockwise). Flush the toilet to empty the tank.
2. Unhook the flapper chain from the flush lever. The flapper lifts out — it's attached to the overflow tube (the vertical pipe in the center of the tank) by two "ears" that hook onto small pegs.
3. Take the old flapper to the hardware store to match the size. Universal flappers (Fluidmaster 502, $8) fit most toilets. But Kohler, Toto, and some American Standard toilets use proprietary flappers ($12-$15). If the universal flapper leaks a small amount (you hear the fill valve kick on briefly every 30-60 minutes), the seal surface on the flush valve is irregular — you need the OEM (original manufacturer) flapper.
4. Install the new flapper (hook the ears onto the pegs, attach the chain to the flush lever with 1/2 inch of slack — just enough for the flapper to fully seat when the lever is at rest, with a tiny bit of slack so the chain isn't holding the flapper open).
5. Turn the water back on. Test flush. Put food coloring in the tank, wait 15 minutes. If no colored water in the bowl, the seal is perfect.

## Problem 2: Phantom Flush — Toilet Randomly Refills by Itself

**Diagnose:** The toilet suddenly "flushes itself" — you hear the tank refilling as if someone flushed, but nobody did. This is a slow leak from the tank into the bowl that gradually lowers the water level, and when it drops below the fill valve's activation point, the fill valve opens to refill the tank.

**Cause:** Same as Problem 1 — a leaking flapper. The flapper leak is slow enough that water trickles into the bowl silently over 30-60 minutes, the tank level drops, and the fill valve kicks on. The fix is identical: replace the flapper.

## Problem 3: Toilet Won't Stop Filling — Water Runs Into the Overflow Tube

**Diagnose:** The tank fills, the water reaches the top of the overflow tube (the vertical pipe), and keeps running — water is pouring into the overflow tube continuously.

**Cause:** The fill valve is not shutting off when the tank is full. This is either: (a) the float is set too high — the water level reaches the overflow tube before the float triggers the shutoff, or (b) the fill valve itself has failed and won't close.

**Fix A — Adjust the float (2 minutes, $0):** The float is a cylindrical plastic piece that slides up and down on the fill valve shaft (Fluidmaster 400A style) or a ball on an arm (older style). When the water level rises, the float rises and closes the fill valve. If the float is set too high, adjust it DOWNWARD.
- Fluidmaster-style (cylinder float): Pinch the metal spring clip on the adjustment rod and slide the float down 1/2 inch. Flush and check the water level — it should stop 1/2 to 1 inch below the top of the overflow tube. The water level is marked by a line on the inside of the tank (on most toilets) or the top of the overflow tube should have a "WATER LEVEL" marking.
- Ball-float style: Bend the metal float arm downward slightly to lower the float position.

**Fix B — Replace the fill valve (20 minutes, $15):** If adjusting the float doesn't stop the running, the fill valve diaphragm has failed (mineral deposits, chlorine damage, or age). Replacement:
1. Turn off the water supply. Flush to empty the tank. Sponge out remaining water.
2. Unscrew the water supply line from the bottom of the fill valve (under the tank, outside). Have a small bowl ready — about 1 cup of water will drain.
3. Unscrew the plastic lock nut that secures the fill valve to the tank (under the tank). The entire fill valve lifts out through the top of the tank.
4. Install a Fluidmaster 400A universal fill valve ($12). It fits every toilet. Adjust the height: the top of the fill valve should be 1-2 inches above the top of the overflow tube. The instructions are printed on the box.
5. Connect the included refill tube (a small flexible hose) from the fill valve to the overflow tube — this tube squirts a small amount of water down the overflow tube during refill to refill the bowl. Secure it with the included clip.
6. Reconnect the water supply line. Turn on the water. Adjust the float for the correct water level (1/2 to 1 inch below the top of the overflow tube).

## Problem 4: Toilet Leaks at the Base — Water on the Floor

**Diagnose:** Water pooling around the base of the toilet. This is either: (a) condensation on the tank (summer, high humidity — harmless), (b) a failed wax ring seal between the toilet and the floor flange, or (c) a crack in the toilet bowl or tank.

**Fix (1-2 hours, $10):**
1. Confirm it's the wax ring: dry the floor completely. Flush the toilet. If water appears at the base during/after the flush, it's the wax ring. If water appears continuously (no flushing), check the supply line connection at the fill valve — it may be dripping and running down the outside of the tank to the floor.
2. Replace the wax ring: turn off water, flush to empty, sponge out remaining water. Disconnect the supply line. Remove the two nuts holding the toilet to the floor bolts (the decorative caps at the base pop off with a flat screwdriver). Lift the toilet straight up (it's heavy — 60-90 lbs. Get help. Set it on an old towel on the floor).
3. Scrape off the old wax ring from the bottom of the toilet and the floor flange with a putty knife. The wax is messy — wear disposable gloves.
4. Install a new wax ring ($5) or a wax-free rubber seal ($10, Fluidmaster Better Than Wax). The rubber seal is reusable if you ever need to remove the toilet again and doesn't compress permanently. Press it firmly onto the floor flange, or onto the bottom of the toilet (follow package instructions).
5. Lower the toilet straight down onto the flange bolts. Do NOT slide or twist it — this smears the wax unevenly. Sit on the toilet (seriously) to compress the wax evenly with your body weight.
6. Tighten the nuts onto the floor bolts. Snug, not tight — overtightening cracks the porcelain toilet base, which cannot be repaired (toilet becomes a $150 replacement).
7. Reconnect the supply line. Turn on water. Flush 3-4 times. Check for leaks at the base.

## Problem 5: Toilet Is Clogged

- **Plunger ($8):** Use a toilet plunger with a flange (the extended rubber piece at the bottom) — NOT a flat sink plunger. The flange creates a seal in the toilet drain. Firm, rapid pushes — you're creating hydraulic pressure, not just splashing water.
- **Toilet auger ($25):** If plunging fails, a toilet auger (also called a closet auger) is a 3-6 foot flexible steel cable inside a rigid tube with a hand crank. The tube protects the porcelain from the cable. Feed the cable in, crank to break up or retrieve the clog. This is what plumbers use — it clears 90% of toilet clogs without removing the toilet.
- **Chemical drain cleaners:** NEVER use Drano or similar in a toilet. The chemicals can: crack the porcelain from heat reaction, damage the wax ring and PVC pipes, and create a hazardous chemical soup that splashes back at you if plunging doesn't work. Toilet clogs are mechanical (an object), not chemical (grease/hair like in sink drains). Mechanical clogs need mechanical solutions.
