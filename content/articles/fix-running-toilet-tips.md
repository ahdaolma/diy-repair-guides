## Do the Food Coloring Test First

![Toilet tank components labeled for repair](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.jLd4cdfUnHQ6Kw8fP_yMrwHaEh%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=3beb8dc8a879f9f3f978bdda912f1615ceb7b79f79cd41bdbe211eaaacf5b567&ipo=images)

Before you buy any parts, determine whether water is leaking from the tank into the bowl. Put 3–5 drops of food coloring (or a toilet dye tablet, available at hardware stores) into the tank water. Wait 15 minutes without flushing. If colored water appears in the bowl, the flapper is leaking. If the tank water stays clear in the bowl but the toilet still runs, the problem is in the fill valve—water is overflowing into the overflow tube. This 15-minute test with $0.02 worth of food coloring saves you from replacing the wrong part.

## Match the Flapper Exactly

![Close up of a new toilet flapper](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.CTRhJ_e8sttI3JLKQqJF3gHaHa%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=d765bb0676ab7a98233ea29e8d5c579bd3c2ae8242206dadf2cf1d5c5d4d9484&ipo=images)

Flappers are not universal despite what the package says. A "universal" flapper may fit physically but seal poorly, causing a slow leak you will not notice until the next water bill. The critical dimension is the flush valve opening diameter—either 2 inches (older toilets) or 3 inches (most toilets manufactured after 2000, designed for 1.6 GPF). Measure before you go to the store. If possible, buy the OEM (original equipment manufacturer) flapper for your toilet brand. Kohler toilets use a proprietary canister-style flush valve that requires a Kohler-specific seal, not a standard flapper. If your toilet has a tower-style flush valve (vertical cylinder instead of hinged flapper), you need the specific seal for that tower.

## Avoid In-Tank Cleaning Tablets

Those blue drop-in cleaning tablets that sit in the tank are convenient but destroy rubber components. The chlorine and bleach compounds continuously degrade the flapper, gaskets, and seals. A flapper that would normally last 5 years may fail in 6 months with drop-in tablets. The worst-case scenario: the degraded flapper disintegrates into sticky black goo that fouls the flush valve seat and requires scraping to remove. If you want automatic bowl cleaning, use an in-bowl clip-on cleaner that does not contact tank components. Better yet, clean the bowl manually with a toilet brush and cleaner—it takes 30 seconds and does not destroy $10–30 worth of internal parts.

## Adjust the Float Before Replacing the Fill Valve

![Toilet fill valve and float assembly](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.35ommPRg2QNnUsz9rP3BrQHaHa%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=8912eb0661e0a53493bece5d29ad01e44855f5fe487fcd528cc02efc4fa8360e&ipo=images)

If water runs continuously into the overflow tube, the simplest and most common fix is adjusting the float, not replacing the fill valve. For modern cup-style fill valves (Fluidmaster 400A and similar), pinch the adjustment clip on the side of the float and slide it down about half an inch. For older ball-and-arm floats, bend the metal rod downward slightly. Flush and observe: the water should stop rising about ½ inch below the top of the overflow tube. If the fill valve still does not shut off after adjusting the float to its lowest position, then and only then replace the fill valve. This tweak costs nothing and takes 30 seconds.

## Clean the Flush Valve Seat When Replacing the Flapper

Every time you replace a flapper, take 30 extra seconds to run your finger around the flush valve seat. If it feels gritty, rough, or has raised mineral deposits, the new flapper will not seal. Clean the seat with fine steel wool (0000 grade) or a nylon scouring pad—just enough to smooth the surface, not enough to scratch the plastic. Wipe the seat clean with a cloth before installing the new flapper. On hard-water systems, mineral buildup is the reason flappers fail prematurely. Clean the seat with each flapper replacement and you will double the life of the new flapper.

## Replace the Supply Line While You Are in There

If your toilet uses a rigid supply line (chrome-plated copper or brass) or an old braided stainless line that is more than 10 years old, replace it with a new braided stainless steel supply line when you replace the fill valve. The supply line costs $6–8 and the fill valve replacement requires disconnecting it anyway. Rigid lines are unforgiving—if they are even slightly misaligned during reconnection, they leak. Braided stainless lines are flexible, seal reliably with hand-tightening plus a quarter-turn, and have a rubber gasket that does not require Teflon tape. A new supply line also eliminates the risk of a 10+-year-old rubber washer failing after you finish the repair and close up the bathroom.

## Know When the Entire Toilet Is the Problem

If your toilet is pre-1994 (using 3.5+ gallons per flush), every component inside it is likely reaching end of life simultaneously. You can replace parts piecemeal—first the flapper, then the fill valve, then the handle, then the flush valve—spending $40–$60 over a year of chasing leaks. Or you can install a new WaterSense-certified 1.28 GPF toilet for $99–$250 that saves 12,000–20,000 gallons per year per toilet compared to pre-1994 models. The water savings alone pay for the new toilet within 2–3 years. If you are on a septic system, a new low-flow toilet also reduces the hydraulic load on your drain field.
