## Why Floors Squeak
![Squeaky hardwood floor being repaired](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.gdGUhXKSVAaIBIzjqubwIwHaFw%3Fr%3D0%26cb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=7748ab68382112ad3109e4b6260d894ff970b42537d943373c4367b962bd99d1&ipo=images)

A floor squeaks when two surfaces rub against each other underfoot. In 90% of cases, this is the subfloor (plywood or OSB) moving against the floor joist because the original nails have loosened. Wood expands and contracts with seasonal humidity changes—a ⅛-inch movement over years is enough to loosen thousands of nails. When you step on the floor, the subfloor flexes down, the nail shaft slides against the wood, and you hear the squeak. Less commonly, hardwood floorboards rub against each other at the tongue-and-groove joints.

Fixing a squeak means eliminating the movement between these two surfaces. There are four approaches, depending on access: from above (through the finished floor), from below (through an unfinished basement or ceiling), with adhesive, or with lubricant for edge squeaks.

## Locate the Squeak Precisely

Have someone walk over the squeaky area while you watch and listen. The sound often travels along the joist—the actual loose nail may be 2–3 feet from where you perceive the squeak. Mark the exact spot with painter's tape. If you have access from below, go underneath and have the person above walk again. You will see the subfloor lift slightly off the joist at the squeak point. Mark the joist and subfloor with a pencil.

## Method 1: From Above With Carpet (Breakaway Screw Kit)

![Squeak No More breakaway screw kit](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.wMi9yOSyptUzhDU8QlLBhAHaHa%3Fr%3D0%26cb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=811ddb165abe186ac9b1266f43313cde604b00894b5b4d20aa7da7bd72353319&ipo=images)

The Squeak No More kit (or Squeeeeek-No-More, $20–25) is specifically designed for carpeted floors. It contains scored screws that snap off below the carpet surface and a depth-control fixture.

1. Locate the floor joist under the squeak. Use a stud finder with "deep scan" mode—it detects the joist through carpet, pad, and subfloor. Alternatively, measure from a known joist (visible in a floor vent opening) in 16- or 24-inch increments. Mark both edges of the joist.
2. Place the alignment fixture over the squeak location on the joist center line. The fixture has a tripod base and a guide tube for the screw.
3. Insert a scored screw into the fixture and drive it through the carpet into the joist with a drill. The screw head will snap off at the score line when it reaches the correct depth. You will hear a distinct "crack"—this is the screw breaking, not your floor.
4. The broken screw head remains below the carpet surface and is invisible. The threaded portion pulls the subfloor tight against the joist, eliminating the movement that causes the squeak.
5. Drive screws every 6–8 inches along the joist through the squeaky area.

## Method 2: From Above on Hardwood (Trim Screws)

![Floor repair screw being driven through hardwood](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.zQ0DDldc-JrjpbS_O672wwAAAA%3Fr%3D0%26cb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=305ed08996e1ffe029ae91e1b5c5912bf620a77f3485c3e60c3b10c1b1fc4509&ipo=images)

For exposed hardwood, the same principle applies, but you must either hide or fill the screw hole.

1. Drill a pilot hole through the hardwood into the joist. Use a drill bit slightly smaller than your screw diameter. The pilot hole prevents the hardwood from splitting.
2. Drive a 2-inch trim-head screw (small head, designed to countersink) through the pilot hole. Tighten until the screw head is ⅛ inch below the wood surface. You should feel the subfloor pull tight.
3. Fill the hole with wood filler or a color-matched wax fill stick ($3–5). For prefinished hardwood, manufacturers sell touch-up kits with the exact stain color.
4. Alternatively, use a face-nailing approach with 8d (2.5-inch) finish nails. Drive two nails at opposing 45-degree angles through the floorboard into the joist. This "toe-nailing" technique mechanically locks the board. Set the nail heads with a nail set and fill.

## Method 3: From Below (Best Option When Accessible)

If you have an unfinished basement or removable ceiling tiles below the squeaky floor, fixing from below is cleaner and more effective.

1. From below, have someone above walk on the squeak while you watch the subfloor-joist junction. You will see a gap open and close, and likely see a nail shaft moving.
2. For a small gap: Insert a thin wood shim coated with carpenter's glue (Titebond II or III) between the subfloor and the joist. Tap it in gently—just enough to fill the gap and stop movement. Over-shimming can lift the floor above. Let the glue dry for 24 hours before walking heavily on the area.
3. For a larger gap or a loose nail that cannot be shimmed: Drive a 1¼-inch drywall or deck screw through the subfloor into the joist at a slight angle (not straight up—the screw tip could penetrate the finished floor above). Use screws, not nails—screws have threads that grip and will not back out. Place screws every 6–8 inches along the joist through the squeaky section.
4. For a nail that has pulled out: Do not try to hammer it back in—it has already proven it will not hold. Remove it with a cat's paw or pry bar and replace with a screw.
5. For subfloor edge squeaks: Apply a continuous bead of construction adhesive (Loctite PL Premium or similar) along the joist where the subfloor meets it. The adhesive fills the gap and bonds the subfloor to the joist permanently.

## Method 4: Lubricant for Board-to-Board Squeaks

If the squeak is a high-pitched creak between two hardwood boards rather than a deeper structural groan, the tongue-and-groove joints are rubbing against each other.

1. Sprinkle talcum powder, graphite powder (lock lubricant), or powdered soapstone liberally over the squeaky joint.
2. Work the powder into the gap by walking over it repeatedly. The powder lubricates the wood-on-wood contact.
3. Wipe excess powder off the surface.
4. This is a temporary fix (6–12 months) but is completely non-invasive and worth trying before any mechanical method.

## Method 5: Construction Adhesive From Above

For carpeted floors with no below access, there is one more option: inject adhesive from above.

1. Locate the joist as in Method 1.
2. Drill a ⅛-inch hole through the carpet and subfloor at the joist location. The small hole will close when the carpet fibers are fluffed.
3. Inject expanding polyurethane construction adhesive (specifically formulated for subfloors, such as DAP Subfloor Adhesive) through the hole into the gap between subfloor and joist.
4. The adhesive expands to fill the gap, bonds the subfloor to the joist, and stops the movement.
5. Weight the area with heavy objects (books, weights) for 24 hours while the adhesive cures.

## Prevention for New Floors

If you are installing new subfloor or hardwood, preventing squeaks is far easier than fixing them:

- Screw the subfloor to joists, never use nails alone. Use 1⅝-inch subfloor screws or ring-shank nails plus construction adhesive.
- Apply a continuous bead of subfloor adhesive (PL 400 or similar) to the top of each joist before laying subfloor panels. This is code requirement in many jurisdictions and eliminates virtually all future squeaks.
- For hardwood: staple or cleat-nail through the tongue at a 45-degree angle every 6–8 inches along each joist. Face-nail the first and last rows where the nailer cannot reach.
