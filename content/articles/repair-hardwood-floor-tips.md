## The Fingernail Test Tells You Everything

![The Fingernail Test Tells You Everything](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.explicit.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.rBgdPMOSpd78vIcqdXSCEQHaNM%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=3b19a70cc6ae3fd9fdb6bdf9b67df3a5cd41c6808a6c0227e7c6c8e963d402de&ipo=images)


Run your fingernail across the scratch. If it glides over smoothly, only the clear finish is damaged—a touch-up marker or wax stick fixes it. If your nail catches, the scratch is into the wood—it needs stain or filler. If your nail drops into a depression, it is a gouge that needs wood filler. This 2-second test tells you which repair method to use and prevents the common mistake of applying stain to a finish-only scratch, which creates a dark line more visible than the original scratch.

## Pre-Tint Wood Filler Before Applying

![Pre-Tint Wood Filler Before Applying](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.H8BWwbLrbXPHMudbHIk42wHaDC%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=ce4ba31bbb02bc7266499fa864e836a31137f388be8e9864b72b5c864903ce35&ipo=images)


"Stainable" wood filler accepts stain but never matches surrounding wood exactly because filler and wood absorb stain at different rates. For the closest match, squeeze a small amount of filler onto a scrap of cardboard, add a few drops of the stain you will use, and mix thoroughly. Apply the pre-tinted filler to the gouge. After sanding, the color is already close to the final tone, and the final stain application blends the repair. This extra minute of mixing makes the difference between a repair you point out and one visitors never notice.

## Match the Finish Sheen, Not Just the Color

![Match the Finish Sheen, Not Just the Color](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.hIoZGUbtuGVAygr69KLNNQHaE7%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=7ed9fd255650ac815f384e8d0479a54a06befcac92025aa74460874329c31e66&ipo=images)


A repair that matches the wood color perfectly but uses gloss polyurethane on a satin floor is instantly visible from across the room. The sheen (gloss, semi-gloss, satin, matte) refracts light differently. Test the sheen on a hidden area: apply the polyurethane to a piece of cardboard or inside a closet, let it dry, and compare it to the floor from multiple angles under natural and artificial light. Satin is the most common pre-finished hardwood sheen, but verify before buying.

## Sand With the Grain Only

Sanding across the grain leaves scratches that stain absorbs deeply, creating dark cross-grain lines that are impossible to remove without more sanding. Always sand parallel to the wood grain, using a sanding block (not your fingers—finger pressure is uneven and creates depressions). Start with 150-grit to level the filler, finish with 220-grit for smoothness. After sanding, wipe with a tack cloth, not a damp rag—water raises the wood grain and can reactivate wood bleach residues.

## Know When Refinishing Beats Spot Repairing

If the floor has more than one major repair per 10 square feet, scattered scratches throughout, or the existing finish is worn through to bare wood in traffic areas, spot repairs will leave a visible patchwork. At this point, sanding and refinishing the entire floor ($3–$5 per square foot professionally, $1–$2 DIY with rented equipment) produces a uniform result that spot repairs cannot match. A floor sander rental costs $60–$80 per day. The sanding removes approximately 1/32 inch of wood—solid hardwood can be refinished 4–6 times in its lifespan. Engineered hardwood with a thin veneer wear layer (under 2mm) can typically be refinished once, if at all—check with the manufacturer before sanding engineered flooring.
