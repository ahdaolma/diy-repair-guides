## Why Fixing a Leaky Faucet Matters
![Leaky faucet being repaired](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthfvnext.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.SL3MZSiwcy01ksYxzcbnnQHaEK%3Fcb%3Dthfvnextfalcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=a1c7d609c0947e4da30a8d137bf68d085659e84b0d992f74ae242e2dfacdb517&ipo=images)

A faucet that drips once per second wastes approximately 3,000 gallons of water per year, according to the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency's WaterSense program. That is 180 showers worth of water, and at the national average water rate of roughly $0.004 per gallon, it costs about $12 annually—more than the parts to fix it. If the drip is hot water, you are also paying to heat water that goes straight down the drain.

The good news: fixing a leaky faucet is one of the most approachable DIY plumbing repairs. It requires no special skills, no pipe cutting, and no soldering. With $5–$25 in replacement parts and 30–60 minutes, you can permanently silence the drip. This guide covers all four common faucet types: compression, cartridge, ball, and ceramic disc.

## Safety First: What You Need

![Faucet repair tools and parts](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthfvnext.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.RV9hjet90-db2fycAeAJZQHaEK%3Fcb%3Dthfvnextfalcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=2580afbd49b6a58f3c08b0231af3183de2e68a35de25915b66ebb6cc66a02c67&ipo=images)

### Tools
- Adjustable wrench or channel-lock pliers
- Flathead and Phillips screwdrivers
- Allen wrench set (hex keys)
- Needle-nose pliers
- Utility knife (for removing decorative caps)
- Penetrating oil (WD-40 or similar, for stuck parts)
- White vinegar (for cleaning mineral deposits)
- Plumber's grease (silicone-based, not petroleum-based)

### Materials
- Replacement parts specific to your faucet (washer, O-ring, cartridge, or ceramic disc)
- Plumber's tape (Teflon tape)

Before you start, close the sink drain or cover it with a rag. Small parts—screws, washers, clips—have a magnetic attraction to open drains. Also, turn off the water supply. Look under the sink for two shutoff valves (hot and cold). Turn both clockwise until they stop. Then open the faucet to release pressure and confirm the water is off. If the valves are stuck or your sink has no individual shutoffs, you will need to close the main water supply to the house.

## Identify Your Faucet Type

![Faucet cartridge and internal components](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthfvnext.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.iSeQG9Rgsaj9YcWd0tW2lgHaHa%3Fcb%3Dthfvnextfalcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=b5fdb3ae801da8371422b6288e1caa0bba1ba4360f6da835451eb325db1b5c38&ipo=images)

The repair procedure depends entirely on which type of faucet you have. Here is how to identify each:

### Compression Faucet (oldest type, most common in older homes)
- Two separate handles (hot and cold)
- Handles turn multiple full rotations to open/close
- Most likely cause of leak: worn rubber washer at the base of the stem
- Also check: O-ring on the stem, corroded valve seat

### Cartridge Faucet (common in bathrooms, 1970s–present)
- One or two handles
- Handles turn 90 degrees or half-turn to open/close
- Smooth operation, no grinding
- Most likely cause of leak: worn cartridge (entire unit), or O-rings on the cartridge body

### Ball Faucet (single-handle kitchen faucets, common in Delta brand)
- Single handle that pivots on a ball joint
- Handle moves up/down for flow, left/right for temperature
- Most likely cause of leak: worn rubber seats and springs under the ball, or worn cam and packing
- Delta sells a repair kit (RP3614 or similar) with all wearing parts

### Ceramic Disc Faucet (modern, high-end)
- Single lever handle
- Smooth, precise operation
- Very reliable; rarely leaks
- Most likely cause of leak: cracked ceramic disc (from freezing or impact), or worn neoprene seals under the cartridge

## Repairing a Compression Faucet

This is the most common leak in older homes and the easiest fix.

1. **Remove the handle.** Pry off the decorative cap on top of the handle (it may have a "H" or "C" indicator) with a flathead screwdriver or utility knife. Remove the screw underneath. Pull the handle straight up. If it is stuck, use a handle puller ($8–12) rather than prying—prying can crack the porcelain or chrome finish.

2. **Remove the stem.** Use an adjustable wrench to unscrew the packing nut (the hex nut the stem passes through). Turn counterclockwise. Once loose, lift the entire stem assembly out of the faucet body.

3. **Replace the washer.** At the bottom of the stem, a brass screw holds a rubber washer. Remove this screw. The washer will be flat, grooved, or disintegrated. Take the old washer to the hardware store to match the exact size—washers come in dozens of diameters and thicknesses. Replace with an exact match. A washer that is slightly too large will bind; too small will not seal.

4. **Replace the O-ring.** On the stem body, there is one or more rubber O-rings that seal against the faucet body. Slide the old O-ring off, coat the new one with plumber's grease, and slide it on. O-rings harden and flatten with age—if yours is not round in cross-section, it needs replacement.

5. **Inspect the valve seat.** Look down into the faucet body at the brass ring the washer presses against. If it feels rough, pitted, or grooved when you run a fingertip over it, a new washer will fail quickly. Use a seat-dressing tool ($10–15) to grind the seat smooth. Turn the tool by hand—you only need to remove a few thousandths of an inch of metal.

6. **Reassemble.** Insert the stem, tighten the packing nut (snug, not overtight—brass threads strip easily), reattach the handle, and replace the decorative cap. Turn the water on slowly and test.

## Repairing a Cartridge Faucet

1. **Remove the handle.** Pry off the index cap, remove the handle screw, and pull the handle off. On some models, an Allen screw on the side of the handle secures it.

2. **Remove the retaining nut or clip.** Above the cartridge, there will be either a threaded retaining nut or a U-shaped metal retaining clip. If it is a nut, unscrew it. If it is a clip, pull it straight up with needle-nose pliers. Do not lose the clip—it is small and easily dropped down the drain.

3. **Remove the cartridge.** Grasp the cartridge stem with pliers and pull straight up. It may resist—years of mineral deposits can cement it in place. A cartridge puller tool ($12–20) threads onto the stem and uses mechanical advantage to extract stubborn cartridges. Do not twist aggressively; you can break the cartridge stem off in the faucet body, which turns a 10-minute job into a major repair.

4. **Match the replacement.** Take the old cartridge to the store. Bring photos of the faucet if you do not know the brand. Major manufacturers (Moen, Delta, Kohler, American Standard, Pfister) use proprietary cartridges. Moen cartridges (model 1225 for older faucets, 1222 for Posi-Temp shower valves) are among the most commonly replaced. Many manufacturers offer free replacement cartridges under lifetime warranty—call their customer service line with your model number before buying.

5. **Install the new cartridge.** Coat the O-rings with plumber's grease. Align the cartridge with the notches in the faucet body (it only goes in one way) and push it firmly into place. Reinstall the retaining clip or nut. Reattach the handle.

## Repairing a Ball Faucet

Ball faucets have multiple small parts that wear together. Do not replace individual pieces—buy the complete repair kit for your brand.

1. **Remove the handle.** Loosen the Allen screw on the side of the handle, pull the handle off.

2. **Remove the cap and collar.** Unscrew the chrome cap and collar assembly.

3. **Remove the cam, packing, and ball.** Lift out the plastic cam (with the adjusting ring) and the rubber packing seal. Then lift out the ball. Note the orientation—there is a small slot or alignment pin.

4. **Replace seats and springs.** Use needle-nose pliers or a small flathead screwdriver to remove the two rubber seats and the springs underneath them. These are seated in holes in the faucet body. Install the new springs (wider end down), then press the new rubber seats on top. This is the most common cause of drips—the rubber seats wear and the springs lose tension.

5. **Install new ball.** The kit includes a new ball. Align the slot and press it onto the seats.

6. **Install new cam and packing.** Place the new rubber packing seal, then the plastic cam. The adjusting ring threads onto the faucet body to secure everything. Tighten until the handle moves with slight resistance but does not feel loose.

## Repairing a Ceramic Disc Faucet

Ceramic disc faucets rarely leak from the discs themselves (the discs are harder than most things that can get into your water). Leaks are usually from the neoprene seals underneath the cartridge.

1. **Remove the handle.** Typically secured by an Allen screw. Remove the handle, then the escutcheon cap.

2. **Remove the cartridge.** Unscrew the mounting screws (usually 2–3 brass screws) and lift the cartridge out.

3. **Replace seals.** On the bottom of the cartridge, there are neoprene or rubber seals (usually 2–3). These flatten over time and allow water to seep past. Replace all seals, not just the one that looks worn. Coat new seals with plumber's grease.

4. **If discs are cracked.** Ceramic discs can crack if the faucet froze or was struck hard. If you see cracks, replace the entire cartridge. Ceramic discs are not individually replaceable in most consumer faucets.

5. **Reassemble.** Tighten mounting screws evenly (alternating pattern, like lug nuts). Do not overtighten—ceramic is brittle.

## When to Call a Plumber

Most faucet leaks are straightforward DIY fixes. Call a plumber if:
- The shutoff valves under the sink are frozen or broke when you tried to turn them
- The faucet body is cracked or corroded (replacement requires cutting into the wall or countertop)
- The cartridge stem breaks off during removal (requires specialized extraction tools)
- The faucet is an obscure vintage brand with no available parts

Professional faucet repair typically costs $150–$300 including the service call. DIY repair with a $5–$25 parts kit is one of the highest-ROI home repairs you can make.

## Preventing Future Leaks

- Do not overtighten faucet handles. A gentle turn is all that is needed. Cranking the handle crushes washers and seats, accelerating wear.
- If you have hard water, install a whole-house water softener or an under-sink filter. Mineral deposits accumulate on seals and cartridges, causing them to leak.
- Exercise shutoff valves under sinks twice a year (turn them off and on). Valves that sit untouched for years seize in place, leaving you unable to isolate a leak when you need to.
