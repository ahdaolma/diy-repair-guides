## Drywall Is the Cheapest Material and the Hardest to Master

![DIY drywall taping and mudding](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.NCKS__nZ0WePDf2CYqNNSAHaE8%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=1179e66d4be1ac0a8db26dbf55c45cc6639938fb4fc2de804131223166b3379f&ipo=images)

A 4x8 sheet of 1/2-inch drywall costs $15. The tools to hang and finish it cost $100-$150. Professional drywall hangers charge $2-$4 per square foot installed and finished. So a 12x12 room (480 sq ft of wall + 144 sq ft of ceiling = 624 sq ft) costs $1,250-$2,500 for a pro, versus $150 in drywall + $150 in mud, tape, and screws for DIY. The $800-$2,100 difference is almost entirely the labor of finishing — hanging the panels is the easy 30%. Taping, mudding, and sanding to an invisible finish is the hard 70% that separates DIY from pro work.

This guide covers hanging to Level 4 finish (paint-ready, the standard for residential walls). Level 5 (skim coat over the entire surface, required for high-gloss paint or critical lighting) adds 25% more time and is rarely needed in residential settings.

## Hanging: The 30% That's Easy If You Plan

![How to mud and tape drywall step by step](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.fBuUv4CKPo16GPCl9oFi0AHaEz%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=d1870187bb8acddcc14c81cf2d9f00411e056903a129f545b265c17c07384a1a&ipo=images)

**Materials:** 1/2-inch lightweight drywall ($15/sheet for 4x8), 1-5/8-inch coarse-thread drywall screws ($10 for a 5-lb box, covers ~50 sheets), drywall screw gun or drill with dimpler bit ($10 for the bit).

**Planning is everything:** A sheet of drywall is 48 inches wide — which means it spans four stud bays (studs at 16 inches on center: 16 x 4 = 64 inches... wait, no. 48 inches = 3 bays of 16 inches. A 48-inch sheet spans from the center of one stud to the center of the third stud over: stud 1 center → stud 2 center (16 inches) → stud 3 center (32 inches) → edge of sheet at center of stud 4 (48 inches). So each sheet spans 3 bays and breaks on a stud.)

**Rules of hanging:**
1. **Hang ceilings first.** Ceiling panels rest on the wall panels — the wall panels push up against the ceiling, supporting the edge. If you hang walls first, you're trying to hold a 70-lb sheet of drywall overhead with nothing to rest it on.
2. **Stagger the seams.** Never let four corners of drywall meet at one point — that joint will crack. Offset vertical seams by at least one stud bay between adjacent rows.
3. **No butt joints in the middle of walls.** A butt joint (where two square edges meet, not tapered edges) is the hardest joint to finish invisibly. Plan your layout so tapered edges (the factory beveled edges on the long sides) meet at seams, and butt joints fall behind furniture or in low-visibility corners whenever possible.
4. **Screw every 12 inches on ceilings, every 16 inches on walls.** Screws 3/8 inch from the edge. Screws slightly below the surface — the head dimples the paper without tearing through it. The dimple holds the mud. If the screw tears through the paper, it has zero holding power — remove it and drive a new screw 2 inches away.
5. **Cutouts for outlets and windows:** Measure from the edge of the nearest hung sheet to the center of the outlet box. Transfer that measurement to the new sheet. Cut with a drywall saw ($8) or rotary tool ($30). Cut slightly small — you can always enlarge. You can't un-cut.
6. **Lift and position:** Drywall weighs 55 lbs per 4x8 sheet (1/2-inch lightweight). Use a drywall lift ($35 rental for 4 hours) for ceilings — it holds the sheet in place while you screw it. Without a lift, you need two people and a T-brace (two 2x4s nailed into a T-shape to wedge the sheet against the ceiling).

## Taping and Mudding: The 70% That Defines the Job

**Materials:** All-purpose joint compound (green lid, $15 for a 4.5-gallon bucket — one bucket covers ~500 sq ft), paper drywall tape ($6 for a 500-foot roll), 6-inch and 12-inch taping knives ($25 for a set of both), mud pan ($8), sanding sponge ($5) or pole sander with 120-grit screens ($25).

**Three coats. No shortcuts. Each coat must dry completely before the next.**

### Coat 1: Tape (embed the tape in mud)

1. Mix the mud: scoop joint compound into the mud pan and mix with your 6-inch knife until it's the consistency of creamy peanut butter. Straight from the bucket it's too stiff — add a few drops of water and mix.
2. Apply a thin, even bed of mud over the seam with the 6-inch knife. You're filling the tapered edge (the factory bevel) so the tape sits below the surface.
3. Center the paper tape over the seam and press it into the wet mud with your knife, starting at one end and working to the other. Hold the knife at a 45-degree angle and apply firm, even pressure — mud should squeeze out from under the tape on both sides. The tape must be fully embedded with no dry spots or bubbles. Dry tape over a dry seam guarantees the tape peels off.
4. Immediately apply a thin skim coat of mud over the tape with the 6-inch knife, feathering the edges. The coat should be thin enough that you can still see the tape through the mud — you're filling the weave of the paper and starting the feather.
5. Let dry COMPLETELY — 12-24 hours depending on humidity. Do NOT sand between coats 1 and 2 — sanding paper tape tears the paper fibers and creates a fuzzy surface that requires another coat to smooth.

### Coat 2: Fill (widen the joint)

1. Lightly knock down any ridges or high spots with the 6-inch knife — a quick scrape, not a sand.
2. Apply a wider band of mud with the 12-inch knife, centered over the seam. This coat fills the tapered edge so the seam is now flush with (or very slightly above) the panel surface. Feather the edges aggressively — the mud should transition smoothly from the center of the seam to zero thickness at the 10-12-inch width mark.
3. Inside corners: mud one side at a time. Apply mud to one side of the corner, embed the tape, let dry 12 hours. Then mud the other side. Mudding both sides of an inside corner at once is impossible — you'll drag the tape out of the corner with your knife on the second side.
4. Outside corners: install metal or plastic corner bead ($3 per 8-foot piece) with the 1-1/4-inch drywall nails or screws. The corner bead provides a straight, durable edge. Apply mud with the 12-inch knife, feathering 8-10 inches out from the corner on both sides. This takes 2-3 coats — each side of the corner bead creates a different profile, and the mud needs to bridge from the raised bead to the flat wall surface.

### Coat 3: Finish (skim and feather)

1. Thin the mud slightly (a few drops of water) for a smoother application.
2. Apply the final skim coat with the 12-inch knife, feathering 14-18 inches wide. The center of the seam should have virtually zero mud — you're filling the tiny pinholes and knife marks from the previous coat. The edges should be feathered to nothing.
3. After drying, sand with 120-grit on a pole sander. Vacuum all dust from walls, floor, and yourself before priming. Drywall dust is fine enough to remain airborne for hours — wear an N95 mask.
4. **The flashlight test:** Hold a bright work light flat against the wall, shining across the surface. Every bump, ridge, and imperfection casts a shadow. If you see shadows, apply another skim coat. Paint magnifies drywall defects, especially satin or semi-gloss. Flat paint hides minor imperfections — this is why builders spray flat paint on ceilings and walls in new construction.

**Butt joints** (square edge meeting square edge) have no tapered area for the mud to fill, so the finished joint creates a slight hump. The technique: feather the mud 24-30 inches wide on each side of the butt joint to create such a gradual rise that the eye can't perceive it. This takes 4-5 coats instead of 3. This is why pros avoid butt joints in the middle of walls.

## When to Hire a Pro

Hanging drywall is physically demanding (55-lb sheets overhead) but straightforward. Finishing drywall to an invisible Level 5 is genuinely difficult — it takes hundreds of hours of practice. If this is your first time and you're finishing a highly visible wall (living room, hallway, any wall with a window that casts side-light), consider hanging the drywall yourself and paying a finisher $0.75-$1.50 per square foot for taping and mudding only. You save 60% of the labor cost, and the result looks professional. If it's a garage, basement, or closet — DIY the whole thing. The practice on low-visibility walls is how you learn.
