## Why Replace Your Shower Head
![Old shower head being removed with wrench](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.8f2N-ZFqP7nH86rhC08IqwHaDF%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=f93b601c259b35143d7c89d0d9ac7fa2922665587e263fa7d12d72d981b56efa&ipo=images)

Replacing a shower head is the single easiest plumbing upgrade in the house. It requires no pipe cutting, no soldering, no shutting off the house water supply—just unscrewing the old head and screwing on the new one. The entire job takes 10–15 minutes with one tool. Yet according to a survey by Moen, 68% of homeowners have never replaced their shower head despite wanting better water pressure or features. A new shower head costs $15–$80 and can transform a weak, uneven spray into a spa-like experience.

## Tools and Materials

- Adjustable wrench or channel-lock pliers
- Plumber's tape (Teflon tape, white, standard density) — $1–2
- Clean cloth or old toothbrush (for cleaning threads)
- New shower head (standard ½-inch NPT connection fits virtually all U.S. shower arms)

Optional but helpful: penetrating oil (WD-40) if the old head is corroded on, and a strap wrench ($8–12) if you want to avoid scratching the finish on the new head when tightening.

## Step 1: Remove the Old Shower Head
![Applying plumber's tape to shower arm threads](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.LeBEVyzW_g7yyiFH5-UG4wHaEK%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=0f4f136f6aae2790258f16fc271eb07e3877c9dbb4daffbf3c1c448cf2def01d&ipo=images)

1. The shower head screws onto the shower arm—the curved pipe extending from the wall. Grip the shower arm with one hand to stabilize it. This is important: the arm is threaded into a fitting inside the wall. If you twist the arm itself, you risk breaking that fitting, which requires opening the wall to repair.
2. With your other hand (or an adjustable wrench), grip the shower head's connection nut—the hex-shaped fitting just behind the head. Turn counterclockwise to loosen.
3. If the connection is stuck: wrap the nut with a cloth before gripping with pliers to protect the finish. Apply penetrating oil to the joint, wait 5 minutes, and try again. A few drops of white vinegar can also dissolve the mineral deposits that are often gluing the threads together.
4. Once loose, unscrew the head completely by hand.
5. Clean the shower arm threads. Use a cloth or old toothbrush to remove old plumber's tape, mineral deposits, and corrosion from the threads. The threads should be clean metal, not coated in gunk. Good thread preparation is the difference between a leak-free installation and a drip.

## Step 2: Apply Plumber's Tape

Plumber's tape (Teflon tape) is essential—it lubricates the threads and fills the microscopic gaps between male and female threads that would otherwise leak. Without tape, metal-on-metal threaded connections almost always drip.

1. Start with clean, dry threads.
2. Wrap the tape clockwise around the shower arm threads (the same direction you will turn the new head to tighten it). If you wrap counterclockwise, tightening the head will unravel the tape.
3. Apply 3–4 wraps for standard connections. Hold the tape taut and press it into the threads with your thumb as you wrap. Tear or cut the tape cleanly at the end.
4. Do NOT apply tape to the shower arm pipe opening—only to the threads. Tape over the opening can break loose and clog the shower head.

## Step 3: Install the New Shower Head
![New shower head installed on shower arm](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.sQJnyR3N7lvmY2PaX6aWVgHaDy%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=3c5181cc7cdbdfbab9240dd9752a87bb14b8f2ed8f1d9b8d37b8b54b0899971c&ipo=images)

1. Screw the new shower head onto the shower arm by hand, turning clockwise. Thread it on until hand-tight.
2. Tighten with a wrench: grip the connection nut with an adjustable wrench and tighten an additional quarter to half turn. Protect the finish with a cloth between the wrench jaws and the nut. Do not overtighten—tapered pipe threads seal by wedging together, and excessive force can crack the plastic nut (on plastic-bodied heads) or deform the brass fitting.
3. If using a handheld shower head with a hose: connect the hose to the shower arm outlet first, then attach the handheld bracket to the wall (most use adhesive or a suction cup—clean the tile with alcohol before mounting for best adhesion). Connect the handheld wand to the hose.
4. Run the water and check for leaks at the connection. If water drips from the threads, tighten slightly (eighth-turn increments) until the drip stops. If tightening does not stop the leak, remove the head, reapply fresh plumber's tape (3–5 wraps this time), and reinstall. Old or insufficient tape is the cause of nearly all shower head connection leaks.

## Special Cases

**Shower arm replacement:** If you are replacing a corroded or damaged shower arm, unscrew it from the wall fitting (turn counterclockwise). Apply tape to both ends of the new arm (wall side and head side). Thread into the wall fitting by hand and tighten. The arm should end up pointing downward at the correct angle—if it is overtightened and points sideways, you can back it off up to a quarter turn, but more than that requires a different arm length or thread depth.

**Filtered shower heads:** These are larger and heavier than standard heads. Ensure the shower arm is securely anchored in the wall. If the arm flexes or the escutcheon (the chrome plate against the wall) pulls away when you hang the filtered head, the arm fitting inside the wall is loose and needs to be tightened or replaced before the new head is installed. Do not hang a heavy filtered head on a loose arm—it will eventually pull out of the wall.

**Water pressure improvement:** If your shower pressure is poor, the shower head may not be the problem. First, remove the flow restrictor (a small plastic disc or O-ring inside the head connection—check the manual for location). If pressure is still poor, the issue is either a clogged shower arm (flush it by running water without the head attached), old galvanized pipes narrowed by corrosion, or a whole-house pressure problem requiring a pressure booster pump.
