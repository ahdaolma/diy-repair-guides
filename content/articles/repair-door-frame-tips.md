## Diagnose Before You Fix

![Damaged door frame jamb](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.7lm63-O3yDUH7S_KBcNd2QHaDt%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=f9ee3af3e9286d85f3863463432fa6342e9c1155154965af5edb18ec9c015541&ipo=images)

Door frame damage falls into four categories, each with a different fix. Get the diagnosis right or you will fix the wrong thing.

**Stripped screw holes (60% of door frame problems):** The door sags, scrapes the floor, or will not latch because hinge screws have pulled out of the jamb. The screw holes are wallowed out—the wood fibers around the screw threads are gone. This is the easiest fix.

**Split jamb (20%):** The jamb wood has cracked, usually along the grain, from someone slamming the door or from a screw driven too close to the edge. A visible crack runs from a screw hole outward.

**Rotted bottom jamb (15%):** Moisture from floor mopping, a leaky threshold, or exterior exposure has rotted the bottom 2-6 inches of the jamb. The wood is soft, dark, and crumbles when probed with a screwdriver.

**Loose strike plate (5%):** The door latches but pops open because the strike plate screws are loose or the plate is misaligned.

## Fix 1: Stripped Hinge Screw Holes (The Toothpick Trick, But Better)

The internet says "stuff toothpicks and wood glue in the hole." This works temporarily, but toothpicks are softwood and will fail again within a year. Here is the permanent fix:

1. Remove the door by tapping the hinge pins out from underneath with a hammer and nail set.
2. Unscrew the hinge from the jamb.
3. Drill out the stripped screw holes with a 3/8-inch drill bit, 1 inch deep.
4. Cut a 3/8-inch hardwood dowel (oak or maple, not pine) to 1-inch lengths. Coat the dowel with Titebond II or III wood glue.
5. Hammer the dowel into the hole. It should be a tight friction fit. Let the glue cure for 2 hours.
6. Cut the dowel flush with the jamb using a flush-cut saw or sharp chisel.
7. Drill a pilot hole (1/8-inch for standard #9 hinge screws) into the center of the dowel.
8. Reattach the hinge with the original screws or, better, 3-inch construction screws (like GRK #9 x 3-inch) that bite into the framing stud behind the jamb.

![Repairing door hinge screw holes](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.VCF0yJw82sSvWhmqJ0SDvgHaFW%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=715aa33caffb8863835686b20c877c2c8ed02445b2d7e3485f504e8ee0b86ed7&ipo=images)

This hardwood-dowel method restores the screw hole to full strength. The 3-inch screws reach the structural framing, making the hinge connection stronger than it was when new. A door fixed this way will not sag again for decades.

## Fix 2: Split Door Jamb

A split jamb means the wood itself has cracked. Wood glue alone will not hold because the split is under tension every time the door opens.

1. Remove the door and hinge from the affected area.
2. Inject Titebond III wood glue deep into the crack. Use a toothpick, a syringe (available at woodworking stores for $3), or compressed air to blow the glue into the crack.
3. Clamp the jamb closed. You need a bar clamp spanning the full jamb width—about 5-6 inches. If you do not own bar clamps long enough, use two 12-inch bar clamps ($8 each at Harbor Freight) or a pipe clamp.
4. After the glue cures (2 hours minimum, overnight preferred), drill a pilot hole through the jamb and into the framing. Drive a 3-inch construction screw through the repaired crack into the stud. This screw prevents the crack from reopening.
5. Fill the screw head with wood filler, sand smooth, and paint.

If the split is wider than 1/8-inch and will not close with clamping pressure, the jamb is too damaged to glue. Cut out the cracked section with an oscillating multi-tool and splice in a new piece of wood (see Fix 3).

## Fix 3: Rotted Bottom Jamb (Dutchman Repair)

![Door frame rot repair](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.Wwafl2kCooaboFf2w_U21QHaEC%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=108165c1b9757280e04d6645871b1d2fce413089a173fef0583cd36f7655e869&ipo=images)

When the bottom of the jamb has rotted, you do not need to replace the entire jamb. A Dutchman repair (splicing in a new piece of wood) is faster and equally strong.

1. Probe the rot with a screwdriver to find where solid wood begins. Mark a horizontal cut line 2 inches above the rotted area.
2. Cut the jamb at your mark line with an oscillating multi-tool. Cut all the way through the jamb to the framing behind it.
3. Remove the rotted section. If it is nailed or screwed to the framing, pry it out.
4. Cut a replacement piece from pressure-treated wood (for exterior doors) or kiln-dried pine (for interior doors) to the exact same dimensions.
5. Apply construction adhesive (Loctite PL Premium) to the back of the new piece and screw it to the framing with 2.5-inch exterior screws.
6. Fill the seam between old and new wood with Bondo Wood Filler (not regular wood filler—Bondo is two-part, does not shrink, and is waterproof). Sand smooth.
7. Prime and paint. The seam is invisible under paint, and the repair outlasts the rest of the jamb.

## Fix 4: Loose Strike Plate

![Door strike plate repair](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.MyhF_9z1nwMcqHX3ZYJ72QHaEI%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=a0227bc14ae9f401314985bc22ff7b7d4816296d033dacaba078fc3b61c51a94&ipo=images)

A loose strike plate lets the door pop open when you lean on it or when the house settles. The fix depends on the cause:

**Screws are loose in the jamb:** Use the same hardwood dowel method as the hinge repair (see Fix 1). Drill out the old screw holes, glue in a 3/8-inch hardwood dowel, drill new pilot holes, and drive 3-inch screws through the strike plate into the framing.

**Strike plate is misaligned (latch hits too high or low):** File the strike plate opening with a metal file to enlarge it. Remove only 1/16-inch at a time—you cannot put metal back. If the misalignment exceeds 1/8-inch, remove the strike plate, fill the old screw holes, and remount the plate in the correct position.

**Door gap is too wide (latch barely catches):** Shim the bottom or middle hinge outward to tilt the latch side of the door closer to the strike plate. A thin cardboard shim (cereal box cardboard) behind the bottom hinge tilts the door slightly and extends the latch reach by about 1/16-inch.

## Wood Filler vs Epoxy: When to Use Which

![Door frame repair materials](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.LykReOGhL_wRuUpus3ZAWgHaHa%3Fcb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=83e31ef895db2508dcb2941e8b5d477367c8276fc99d3a6b6bcd4a7317374641&ipo=images)

**Wood filler (Minwax, Elmer's, DAP):** For cosmetic repairs only. Fill nail holes, small dents, scratches. Do NOT use for structural repairs—wood filler has no structural strength, crumbles under screw pressure, and dissolves if exposed to moisture.

**Two-part wood epoxy (Abatron WoodEpox, J-B Weld WoodWeld):** For structural repairs in dry locations. Sticks to wood, hardens to a sandable, paintable, screwable solid. Use for repairing stripped screw holes when a dowel repair is impractical, or for rebuilding small sections of rotted wood where a Dutchman is overkill.

**Bondo Wood Filler (two-part polyester):** For exterior rot repairs. Waterproof, does not shrink, cures in 15 minutes. Sands to a glass-smooth finish. The go-to for exterior jamb repairs because it does not absorb moisture. Downsides: smells terrible (use in a ventilated area), hardens too fast for large repairs, and requires immediate tooling—you have about 5 minutes of working time.
