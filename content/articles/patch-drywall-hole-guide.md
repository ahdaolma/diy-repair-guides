## Understanding Drywall Repair by Hole Size

![Drywall hole being patched with joint compound](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.mP6echNdbIiFSaPeoj4bbQHaHP%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=1931e9875390404ff2cae71055f8d722ea4a093deba9355d213c88d6bf1138cc&ipo=images)

Drywall damage falls into three categories, each requiring a different repair approach:

- **Small holes (nail holes, screw holes, dings under ½ inch):** Fill with spackle or joint compound. No backing needed. Dry time: 1–2 hours.
- **Medium holes (doorknob punctures, ½ inch to 4 inches):** Require a mesh or metal patch for backing plus joint compound. The compound alone will crack and fall out without reinforcement. Dry time: 24 hours with drying-type compound.
- **Large holes (4 inches and larger, up to roughly 12×12 inches):** Require a new piece of drywall or a California patch. Larger than 12×12 typically requires cutting back to the nearest studs and installing a full drywall patch with screws.

This guide covers all three. The total cost for any of these repairs is under $25 in materials. Professional drywall repair for a single doorknob hole typically costs $75–$150—the materials cost roughly $10, and the rest is labor and the service call minimum.

## Small Holes (Under ½ Inch)

![Applying spackle to a small drywall hole](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.YiiG_uB_6iKuBq3h5e_41QHaHZ%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=0400b13bc36ad1d6dafd12596a1208b10ef181c4977660c37d7bef6c400b46aa&ipo=images)

These are nail holes from picture hangers, screw holes from removed fixtures, and minor dings. The fix takes 5 minutes of active work plus drying time.

1. Remove any loose drywall paper around the hole. Use a utility knife to trim torn paper flush—if you spackle over loose paper, it will bubble and peel later.
2. Fill the hole with lightweight spackle using a putty knife. Press the spackle firmly into the hole to fill it completely, then scrape the surface flush with the wall. A slight depression is acceptable—spackle shrinks slightly as it dries, and a shallow fill is easier to sand smooth.
3. Let it dry completely (1–2 hours for small fills; humidity extends drying). Spackle changes from off-white to bright white when dry.
4. Sand flush with 150-grit sanding sponge. Run your hand over the patch—your fingers detect imperfections your eyes miss.
5. If the patch is not perfectly flush after sanding, apply a second thin coat (feathering it out wider than the first), let dry, and sand again.
6. Prime the patch before painting. Unprimed spackle absorbs paint differently than the surrounding wall, creating a visible dull spot (called "flashing") that no amount of paint will hide.

## Medium Holes (½ Inch to 4 Inches)

These are doorknob punches, removed electrical boxes, and impact damage. The hole is too large for spackle alone—it will sag and crack without backing.

1. Clean up the hole. Trim away any loose or crushed drywall with a utility knife. Cut the hole edges clean and square-ish if they are ragged.
2. Apply a self-adhesive mesh patch over the hole. The patch should extend at least 1 inch beyond the hole edges in all directions. Press the adhesive edges firmly to the wall.
3. Apply the first coat of joint compound (not spackle—joint compound has better adhesion for mesh patches). Use a 6-inch drywall knife. Spread compound over the entire mesh patch with firm pressure to force compound through the mesh and into the hole. Feather the edges outward 2–3 inches beyond the patch. The first coat will look rough. It is supposed to.
4. Let the first coat dry completely—minimum 4 hours, ideally overnight. Joint compound darkens when dry.
5. Apply a second, thinner coat. Extend the feathered edges 4–6 inches beyond the patch to blend the repair into the surrounding wall. Use a wider knife (10–12 inches if you have one) for the smoothest feathering.
6. Let the second coat dry completely.
7. Sand with 150-grit sanding sponge. Use a work light held at a shallow angle to the wall—it casts shadows that reveal every ridge and depression.
8. If imperfections remain after sanding, apply a third extremely thin coat, let dry, and sand. Patience at this stage determines whether the repair is invisible or obvious.
9. Prime and paint. The patch area plus surrounding 6 inches need primer. Then paint the entire wall if possible—spot-painting only the patch leaves visible sheen differences, especially with semi-gloss or eggshell finishes.

## Large Holes (4–12+ Inches): The California Patch

![Finished drywall patch sanded smooth](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse3.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.fPe6PXZyLVbEyteijTnTEwHaHK%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=9bb91714e75bef3a78638561c6aa99351849bdaa4b764a3e15a57dc4d74d9cf2&ipo=images)

The California patch (also called a butterfly patch or hot patch) uses the drywall's own paper facing as the joint tape, creating a seamless repair without mesh or backing boards. It works for holes up to about 12×12 inches.

1. Cut a square or rectangle of new drywall that is 2 inches larger than the hole in both dimensions. For example, for a 6×6 inch hole, cut a 10×10 inch piece.
2. On the back side of this piece, score the drywall 2 inches in from all four edges with a utility knife. Score through the gypsum but not through the front paper.
3. Snap the gypsum along the score lines and peel it off, leaving only the front paper extending 2 inches beyond the gypsum plug on all four sides. The result looks like a drywall plug with paper wings.
4. Test-fit the gypsum plug into the hole. Trim the plug or the hole edges as needed for a snug fit with no more than ⅛ inch gap around the perimeter.
5. Apply a thin layer of joint compound around the edges of the hole and on the back of the paper wings.
6. Press the patch into the hole. The gypsum plug fills the hole, and the paper wings lie flat on the wall surface. Use the drywall knife to smooth the paper flat and squeeze out excess compound from beneath—work from the center outward so compound does not build up under the paper.
7. Let the first coat dry completely (24 hours). The paper must be fully dry before the next coat or it will bubble.
8. Apply two more thin coats of joint compound, each extending 6–8 inches beyond the patch and each drying fully between coats. The final coat should extend 10–12 inches beyond the hole to blend imperceptibly into the wall.
9. Sand, prime, and paint. Large patches need a full wall repaint to hide the repair—spot-painting a 12-inch feathered repair on a sunlit wall will always be visible due to texture and sheen differences.

## For Holes Larger Than 12 Inches

When the hole spans from stud to stud (or is larger than a California patch can cover), you need to install wood backing and screw in a full drywall patch:

1. Cut the hole into a clean rectangle that reaches the center of the nearest studs on both sides. Use a drywall saw or oscillating multi-tool.
2. Cut a piece of drywall to fit the opening exactly.
3. Screw the patch to the exposed studs using 1¼-inch drywall screws every 8 inches.
4. Tape all four joints with paper drywall tape embedded in joint compound. Paper tape is stronger than mesh for long seams and resists cracking better.
5. Apply three coats of joint compound over the tape, each wider than the last and each fully dry before the next.
6. Sand, prime, paint.

## Common Mistakes

- **Skipping primer.** This is the number one reason DIY drywall repairs are visible. Unprimed compound absorbs paint differently, creating a dull, flat spot that catches light differently than the surrounding wall. Always prime.
- **Not removing loose paper.** Torn drywall paper around a hole will bubble when wet compound is applied. Trim it clean with a utility knife.
- **One thick coat instead of multiple thin coats.** Thick compound shrinks, cracks, and takes forever to dry. Three thin coats produce a smoother, stronger result than one thick one.
- **Sanding too aggressively.** You can sand through the paper tape or mesh, exposing the patch and requiring a restart. Use a light touch with 150-grit or higher.
- **Not feathering wide enough.** A patch that is only 2 inches wider than the hole will always be visible as a hump. Feather at least 6–8 inches beyond the patch edges.
