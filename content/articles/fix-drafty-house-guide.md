## Why Air Leaks Cost You Money
![Applying weatherstripping to a door frame](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.MI6gcnobvgyMGr3LJnVedgHaD2%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=9b12dd3f8d71940ca625f7f7a99545c2de57a758f04d678ed736023ce1a88c1c&ipo=images)

The U.S. Department of Energy estimates that air leaks account for 25–40% of the energy used for heating and cooling in a typical home. The cumulative gaps and cracks in an average house add up to the equivalent of leaving a window wide open year-round. Air sealing is the single highest-ROI energy improvement a homeowner can make—the materials cost $50–$150 for an entire house, and the typical payback period is under one year through reduced heating and cooling bills.

The DOE further notes that air sealing plus attic insulation (to R-49 or R-60 in cold climates) can reduce heating and cooling costs by an average of 15% and up to 30% in older homes. Find your recommended insulation level by zip code at the DOE's Insulation Fact Sheet.

## Find the Leaks First
![Using an incense stick to detect window drafts](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.so4vROmIuBfFWF-fXiPRlQHaHP%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=623b536fe7314e774387342c77aad9a03565573ff882fdca186943e2b1364890&ipo=images)

Systematically finding leaks prevents you from guessing and missing major sources. On a cold or windy day (the larger the indoor-outdoor temperature difference, the easier leaks are to detect):

1. Close all windows, doors, and fireplace flues.
2. Turn on all exhaust fans (bathroom, kitchen range hood) to depressurize the house. This pulls outside air through every leak.
3. Walk through the house with a lit incense stick or a smoke pen ($15–20, produces a thin stream of visible smoke). Hold it near suspected leak locations. If the smoke wavers, blows horizontally, or is sucked toward the gap, you have found a leak.
4. Alternatively, use a thermal leak detector ($25–40, an infrared thermometer with a temperature-difference indicator). It shows the surface temperature difference where cold air is infiltrating.

**The most common leak locations in order of total leakage area:**
1. Attic hatch / pull-down stairs (a surprising volume—the equivalent of leaving a medium window open)
2. Basement rim joist (where the foundation meets the wood framing)
3. Plumbing and wiring penetrations through walls and ceilings
4. Recessed can lights (unless ICAT-rated airtight models)
5. Electrical outlets and switches on exterior walls
6. Window and door frames (the gap between the frame and the rough opening, not the window sash itself)
7. Dryer vent, bathroom fan, and kitchen exhaust duct penetrations

## Weatherstripping Doors
![Caulking gap around a window frame](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse1.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.dSh0IFUflh5wI6TB4Ga0cgHaEl%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=59005e5e6dc5a08abfd5872b4bb1b2e72ab66197fc5189e7b76242cea251f691&ipo=images)

Weatherstripping is the flexible material that seals the gap between the door and the frame. It wears out—rubber hardens, foam compresses, and felt mats down—over 3–10 years depending on material and exposure.

**Door sweep (bottom of door):**
- Measures: Gap between door bottom and threshold, typically ¼ to ½ inch.
- Install an aluminum-and-rubber door sweep ($10–15). Screw it to the door face—the rubber fin should press firmly against the threshold across the full width with no visible light underneath. For uneven thresholds, use a sweep with multiple flexible fins.
- For large gaps under interior doors, a simple stick-on foam or vinyl door bottom ($5–8) works.

**Door frame weatherstripping:**
- For wood doors: adhesive-backed foam tape or V-strip (spring bronze or vinyl). Apply to the door stop (the wood strip the door presses against when closed), not the face of the frame. Clean the surface with alcohol first—adhesive will not stick to dust or grease.
- For exterior doors: compression weatherstripping (a tubular rubber or vinyl gasket in an aluminum channel that screws to the door stop). This is more durable than foam tape and seals against warped doors better.
- Foam tape costs $3–5 for 17 feet. Compression gasket weatherstripping costs $15–25 per door but lasts 10+ years.

## Sealing Windows

Window leaks come from two places: the gap between the sash and the frame (operable window parts) and the gap between the window frame and the rough opening (hidden behind trim). The sash gap is what you feel on a windy day. The frame gap requires more work to access.

**Sash gaps:**
- Apply V-strip weatherstripping (vinyl or bronze, $5–8 per roll) in the channels where the sash slides. The V-shape compresses when the window closes, creating a seal.
- For double-hung windows that rattle, install a window sash lock that pulls the sashes tight together. If the existing lock does not draw the sashes snug, replace it with an adjustable-tension lock ($8–12).
- For fixed gaps at the meeting rail (where upper and lower sashes meet), apply adhesive foam tape to one sash edge.

**Frame-to-rough-opening gaps:**
- Remove the window trim (carefully pry off with a flat bar—you will reinstall it).
- Fill the gap between the window frame and the rough opening with low-expansion window and door foam (Great Stuff Window & Door, $5–8 per can). Standard expanding foam exerts enough pressure to bow window frames; window-and-door formula does not.
- If the gap is small (under ¼ inch), caulk with silicone or paintable acrylic caulk.
- Replace the trim and caulk the trim-to-wall joint.

## Outlets and Switches on Exterior Walls

Electrical boxes on exterior walls are uninsulated holes in your wall. On a windy day, you can often feel cold air blowing through the receptacle slots.

1. Turn off the breaker for the circuit.
2. Remove the outlet cover plate.
3. Install a foam outlet gasket ($2–3 for a pack of 10). These are pre-cut foam sheets that fit behind the cover plate. They reduce airflow but do not eliminate it because the box itself is uninsulated.
4. For a more thorough seal: remove the outlet from the box (after verifying power is off), caulk the gaps where wires enter the box, and apply a minimal amount of fire-rated expanding foam or caulk around the box perimeter where it meets the drywall. Do not fill the box interior with foam—it is a fire hazard and makes future electrical work impossible.
5. Install child safety plugs in unused outlets—they reduce airflow through the slots.

## Attic Hatch and Pull-Down Stairs

An uninsulated attic hatch can leak as much air as a chimney. The DOE estimates that a poorly sealed attic hatch in a 1,500-square-foot house leaks approximately 500–1,500 cubic feet of air per minute when the house is depressurized.

1. Weatherstrip the hatch opening with adhesive foam tape on the trim where the hatch door rests.
2. Attach hook-and-eye latches or barrel bolts to pull the hatch door tight against the weatherstripping. The weight of the door alone is insufficient compression.
3. Build or buy an insulated attic stair cover ($40–80). This is a foam or fiberglass box that fits over the folded stair assembly in the attic, creating an insulated and air-sealed cap. For a DIY version, build a box from 1-inch rigid foam board (R-5 per inch) with foil tape at the seams, sized to fit over the stair opening, and weight it down with a scrap of plywood.

## Basement Rim Joist

The rim joist (also called the band joist) is where the floor framing sits on top of the foundation wall. In most homes, it is a cavity between floor joists filled with—at best—a thin piece of fiberglass batt insulation that does nothing to stop air movement. Cold outside air flows through the rim joist into the basement and rises through the house (stack effect).

1. Cut pieces of 2-inch rigid XPS or polyisocyanurate foam board to fit snugly between each joist bay against the rim joist. Cut slightly undersized (¼ inch gap all around).
2. Seal the perimeter of each foam piece with expanding foam or acoustical caulk to create an air seal.
3. If the basement is conditioned space, leave the foam exposed. If you want to cover it, install fiberglass batts over the foam (foam provides the air seal, fiberglass adds R-value).
4. Material cost: approximately $1.50–$2.00 per square foot for the foam and sealant.

## Pipe and Wire Penetrations

Every pipe, wire, and duct that passes through a wall, floor, or ceiling creates an air pathway.

- For gaps around pipes under sinks: fill with expanding foam or stuff with copper mesh (steel wool rusts and stains) and seal with caulk. The copper mesh also deters mice.
- For wire penetrations: caulk or foam.
- For plumbing vent stacks passing through the attic floor: seal the gap between the pipe and the drywall or subfloor with fire-rated caulk or foam.
- For dryer and bath fan ducts: seal the wall or ceiling penetration with foil tape (not duct tape—it dries out and falls off) and mastic sealant.

## After Sealing: Check Combustion Safety

Sealing a house tightly can change the pressure dynamics enough to backdraft combustion appliances (gas water heater, gas furnace, gas stove) that rely on natural draft for venting. After significant air sealing, have a home performance contractor perform a combustion safety test ($150–$300) to verify that gas appliances are still venting properly and that indoor air quality has not been compromised. If backdrafting is detected, a makeup air duct or direct-vent appliance replacement may be needed.
